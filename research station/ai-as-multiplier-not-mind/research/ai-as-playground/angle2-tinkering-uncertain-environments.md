# Angle 2: Play, Tinkering, and Learning in Uncertain Environments — Research Landscape

**Core claim:** Play functions as an epistemology — a way of knowing that works when systematic approaches fail. Because AI's capability frontier is jagged, unpredictable, and shifting, tinkering (not instruction, not best practices) is the epistemologically correct mode for learning to use it.

## 1. Supporting Work

Sources that directly support the thesis that play and tinkering are epistemologically appropriate responses to domains with jagged, unpredictable capability frontiers.

### 1.1 Bricolage: The Tinkerer's Epistemology

**Lévi-Strauss, *The Savage Mind* (1962) — Chapter 1: "The Science of the Concrete"**
The foundational text. Lévi-Strauss contrasts two figures: the *bricoleur* (tinkerer) and the *engineer*. The bricoleur works with "whatever is at hand" — a finite and heterogeneous collection of tools and materials that "bears no relation to the current project" but is "the contingent result of all the occasions there have been to renew or enrich the stock or to maintain it with the remains of previous constructions or destructions." The engineer, by contrast, works through concepts, subordinating each task to "the availability of raw materials and tools conceived and procured for the purpose of the project." The engineer questions the universe; the bricoleur addresses a sub-set of culture.

Crucially, Lévi-Strauss uses bricolage to characterize *mythical thought* — what he calls "the science of the concrete." This is not inferior thinking but a parallel strategic level at which nature is accessible to inquiry: "one roughly adapted to that of perception and the imagination; the other at a remove from it." Both are legitimate. The science of the concrete gave humanity agriculture, animal domestication, pottery, weaving, and fermentation millennia before modern chemistry. The title itself is a pun: *la pensée sauvage* means both "savage thought" and the wild pansy — uncultivated, not deficient.

The bricoleur's first practical step is *retrospective*: interrogating what is already at hand to discover what each element "could signify." The bricoleur works through *signs* rather than *concepts* — signs carrying both sensory image and conceptual use, whereas concepts aspire to transparency. Mythical thought rearranges pre-existing elements into new configurations, "like the bricoleur who constructs things using whatever materials are at hand."

**Application to AI:** The connection is remarkably direct. You cannot engineer your way to AI competence because the capability landscape is irregular and shifts with each model update. The effective AI user is a bricoleur: probing the tool with real tasks, registering where it succeeds and fails, building a personal repertoire of working patterns (prompts, workflows, workarounds) that is the contingent result of all prior engagements. Knowledge accumulates through concrete engagement, not abstract specification. The competence is concrete, contextual, and combinatorial — exactly as Lévi-Strauss described.

**Turkle & Papert, "Epistemological Pluralism and the Revaluation of the Concrete" (Signs, 1990; revised in Journal of Mathematical Behavior, 1992)**
Applied bricolage directly to learning programming. Identified "bricoleur programmers" who understand code through anthropomorphization, physical metaphor, and exploratory tinkering rather than formal, top-down planning. Argued that computing culture exhibited a "discrimination against epistemological orientations," systematically devaluing the concrete, intuitive style — even though both styles produce working programs. This prefigures the AI learning question directly: if bricolage is a legitimate way to learn programming, it is equally legitimate for learning AI systems whose internal logic is even more opaque than source code.

**Baker & Nelson, "Creating Something from Nothing: Resource Construction through Entrepreneurial Bricolage" (Administrative Science Quarterly, 2005)**
The most influential organizational extension of Lévi-Strauss. Studied 29 resource-constrained firms and found that successful entrepreneurs "made do by applying combinations of the resources at hand to new problems and opportunities," exploiting physical, social, and institutional inputs that other firms rejected or ignored. Operationalized bricolage into three components: *resources at hand*, *recombination for new purposes*, and *making do*. This has become standard in organizational research and spawned subtypes: selective bricolage, collective bricolage, institutional bricolage. A 2024 systematic review in Entrepreneurship and Regional Development documents the proliferation.

**Application to AI:** Users who develop effective AI workflows are doing entrepreneurial bricolage — combining prompt techniques, workflow structures, and domain knowledge in novel configurations, making do with whatever model capabilities are at hand rather than waiting for optimal tools.

### 1.2 Constructionism: Learning by Building

**Papert, *Mindstorms: Children, Computers, and Powerful Ideas* (1980)**
Papert's constructionism argues that learning happens most effectively when learners build things — not when they receive instruction. Children learn mathematics not through formal lessons but through building programs with Logo, directing a virtual turtle across the screen. The process of constructing a working artifact forces learners to confront their own misconceptions and develop genuine understanding.

Central concepts:
- **"Messing about"** — unstructured exploration is not wasted time but a necessary phase of learning. Papert borrowed the term from David Hawkins and argued that structured instruction before sufficient messing about produces brittle, transferable-in-name-only knowledge.
- **Microworlds** — simplified environments where learners can experiment freely without real-world consequences. Logo's Turtle Graphics was a microworld for geometric thinking. The key property: immediate feedback from actions, allowing rapid iteration.
- **Objects-to-think-with** — physical or digital artifacts that externalize abstract ideas and make them manipulable. The turtle was an object-to-think-with for geometry.
- **The Samba School metaphor** — Papert compared ideal learning environments to Brazilian samba schools, where novices learn alongside experts through participation, not instruction. Mixed-age, mixed-skill communities where learning happens through doing, watching, and gradually taking on more complex roles.

Papert explicitly positioned constructionism against *instructionism* — the belief that the way to improve learning is to improve instruction. His claim was that the limiting factor is not the quality of teaching but the poverty of the environment for exploration.

**Application to AI:** AI itself is a microworld for exploring knowledge work. When you build something with AI — a draft, a code module, an analysis — you discover what AI can and cannot do through the act of construction, not through reading documentation. The failures and surprises are the primary learning mechanism. Papert's argument predicts that AI tutorials and best practices will be less effective than giving people projects to build with AI — exactly what the evidence shows.

**Resnick, *Lifelong Kindergarten: Cultivating Creativity through Projects, Passion, Peers, and Play* (2017)**
Resnick, Papert's student and creator of Scratch at MIT Media Lab, extends constructionism into the digital age. His "Four P's" framework — Projects, Passion, Peers, Play — describes the conditions under which creative learning flourishes. Play is not a separate phase but a quality of engagement: iterative, experimental, low-stakes, intrinsically motivated. The Scratch community (tens of millions of users) is an existence proof that tinkering communities can develop shared expertise at scale. Resnick's design philosophy of "low floors" (easy entry), "high ceilings" (room for sophistication), and "wide walls" (many paths to expertise) provides a design framework for AI learning environments.

**"Papert's Vision Realized: Constructionism and Generative AI" (Constructionism Conference, 2025)**
A recent paper argues that generative AI represents the evolution from Papert's "objects to think with" to "agents to think with" — interactive learning partners capable of adaptive response and engagement in higher-order thinking. Where Logo offered deterministic responses to commands, AI offers genuine dialogue and adaptive responsiveness, creating richer constructionist possibilities. The paper contends that "the convergence of artificial intelligence with constructionist learning theories is bringing forth an era where human-AI collaboration fosters the co-evolution of knowledge."

**"Advancing Constructionism Across Three Digital Epochs" (arXiv, 2025)**
Traces the constructionist lineage from Logo through Scratch to generative AI, arguing that AI tools represent a new class of "objects to think with" that support constructionist learning when users engage with them playfully rather than instrumentally. Each digital epoch expands the microworld's expressive power while preserving the core principle: learning through building.

### 1.3 Craftsmanship: Skill Through Resistant Material

**Sennett, *The Craftsman* (2008)**
Sennett argues that skilled practice develops through sustained physical engagement with *resistant* materials — not through abstract knowledge. The book's central metaphor is a dialogue between hand and material, a tactile conversation in which the material "pushes back," offering resistance, surprises, and guidance.

Key concepts:
- **Material consciousness** — an intuitive awareness of how materials behave, what they can and cannot do, and how they respond to intervention. Not theoretical knowledge but embodied judgment. This is the difference between *handling* clay and *understanding* it.
- **Problem-finding vs. problem-solving** — Sennett describes "the intimate, fluid join between problem solving and problem finding" as central to craftsmanship. The craftsman does not merely execute solutions to known problems but discovers new problems through engagement. Three underlying abilities: to *localize* (identify where something matters), to *question* (interrogate assumptions), to *open up* (discover latent possibilities).
- **The 10,000-hour reframe** — The widely cited notion is misleading if taken as mere time accumulation. What matters is the quality of engagement with resistant material — willingness to confront difficulty, ambiguity, and failure as sources of learning.
- **Skill builds irregularly** — "Skill builds by moving irregularly, and sometimes by taking detours." Progress is not linear. Obstacles and ambiguity are not failures of the process but constitutive of it.
- **Domain shifts** — applying techniques from one material/context to another generates productive mismatches that force adaptation and discovery.
- **Play as foundation** — Sennett connects craftsmanship directly to play: "craftsmanship draws on what children learn through play's dialogue with physical materials." The playful dimension of skilled work is not a luxury but a foundation.

**Application to AI:** The resistance AI provides — hallucinations, refusals, unexpected outputs, subtle reasoning errors — functions exactly as material resistance does in Sennett's framework. These failures are not annoyances but *teachers*. Each encounter refines the user's mental model. The skilled AI user develops "material consciousness" for AI: an intuitive sense of what it can and cannot do, built through hands-on engagement. Sennett's emphasis on problem-finding maps directly: the skilled AI user develops the ability to localize (which parts of a problem suit AI), question (interrogate outputs rather than accepting them), and open up (discover unexpected capabilities through exploratory use).

### 1.4 Exploration vs. Exploitation: The Mathematics of Tinkering

**March, "Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational Learning" (Organization Science, 1991)**
One of the most-cited papers in organizational studies. March defines two fundamental activities: *exploration* (search, variation, experimentation, risk-taking, discovery, innovation) and *exploitation* (refinement, efficiency, implementation, execution, selection). The central insight: exploitation tends to drive out exploration because exploitation provides more immediate, certain returns. Organizations systematically under-explore.

The mechanism: early success with one approach (exploitation) generates positive feedback that makes further exploitation more attractive, while exploration's returns are uncertain, distant, and distributed across many actors. "The certainty, speed, proximity, and clarity of feedback ties exploitation to its outcomes more quickly and more tightly than is the case with exploration." The result is a competency trap — organizations become increasingly competent at increasingly obsolete practices.

March argues that maintaining an appropriate balance between exploration and exploitation is a primary factor in system survival and prosperity. Too much exploration produces "too many undeveloped new ideas and too little distinctive competence." Too much exploitation produces rigidity and obsolescence.

**Application to AI:** Organizations that jump immediately to "AI best practices" and standardized workflows are exploiting before they've explored — March's warning made concrete. The METR study (experts 19% slower with AI while believing they were 20% faster) is what happens when you exploit without exploring: confident deployment of an approach that hasn't been tested against the jagged frontier. Mollick's Self-Automators (27% of BCG consultants who handed work entirely to AI) are the extreme exploitation case — and they lost both AI skills and domain skills. The Cyborgs (60%, continuous iterative dialogue) represent the exploration-exploitation balance: using AI but continuously probing and adjusting.

**The Multi-Armed Bandit Problem**
The mathematical formalization of explore-vs-exploit. When facing slot machines with unknown payoff distributions, how do you balance trying new machines (exploring) vs. playing the one that's paid best so far (exploiting)? Strategies include epsilon-greedy (exploit most of the time, explore randomly with probability epsilon), Upper Confidence Bound (explore machines with high uncertainty in their payoff estimates), and Thompson sampling (probabilistic exploration proportional to the likelihood each arm is optimal).

The key insight for AI: when the payoff landscape is unknown and jagged, exploration is *mathematically* optimal, not merely psychologically comfortable. The more uncertain you are about the landscape, the more you should explore. Since AI's capability frontier is empirically jagged and shifts with each model update, the mathematical argument favors extensive exploration (play) before convergence on routines (exploitation).

### 1.5 Cynefin: Probe-Sense-Respond in Complex Domains

**Snowden, "The Cynefin Framework" (originally developed 1999 at IBM Global Services; published as Snowden & Boone, "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making," Harvard Business Review, 2007)**
Cynefin (Welsh for "habitat") classifies problem domains by the relationship between cause and effect:

| Domain | Cause-Effect | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Clear/Simple | Obvious, repeatable | Sense-categorize-respond (best practice) |
| Complicated | Discoverable through analysis | Sense-analyze-respond (good practice, requires expertise) |
| Complex | Emergent, only visible in retrospect | Probe-sense-respond (emergent practice) |
| Chaotic | No discernible relationship | Act-sense-respond (novel practice) |

In the **complex domain**, "instructive patterns can emerge if the leader conducts experiments that are safe to fail." You cannot analyze your way to understanding; you must probe (try something), sense (observe what happens), respond (adjust). This is fundamentally a play-like epistemology — exploratory, iterative, learning from outcomes rather than predicting them.

**Application to AI:** The jagged frontier places AI squarely in the complex domain. You cannot predict which tasks AI will handle well and which it will botch — the relationship between input and output quality is emergent, not analyzable in advance. Best practices (Clear domain thinking) and expert analysis (Complicated domain thinking) both fail because the frontier is irregular and shifting. The appropriate response is probe-sense-respond: try a task with AI, observe the result, adjust your approach. This is tinkering. This is play. Organizations that treat AI adoption as a Complicated problem (hire experts, develop best practices, train staff) are using the wrong framework for a Complex domain.

## 2. Challenging Work

Sources that complicate, push back against, or qualify the thesis that play/tinkering is sufficient for learning AI.

### 2.1 Deliberate Practice: The Case for Structure

**Ericsson & Pool, *Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise* (2016); Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer, "The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance" (Psychological Review, 1993)**
Ericsson's deliberate practice framework argues that expertise develops not through repetition or play but through structured, effortful practice with specific characteristics: well-defined goals, immediate feedback, full concentration, and activity at the edge of current ability. Three levels: *naive practice* (mere repetition — quickly plateaus), *purposeful practice* (adds goals and feedback but is self-directed), and *deliberate practice* (adds expert guidance and activities specifically designed to improve performance).

The central mechanism is the development of increasingly sophisticated *mental representations* — internal models that allow practitioners to monitor their own performance, detect errors, and self-correct. Developing these representations is the primary purpose of deliberate practice.

**The challenge to the play thesis:** If expertise requires structured, guided practice, then unstructured tinkering may build superficial familiarity without developing the deep mental representations needed for genuine skill. Play might map the surface of the frontier without developing the judgment to navigate it. The "vibe coding" phenomenon (10-100x creation, 0.4-0.6x quality) could be interpreted as play without the structure that deliberate practice provides.

**Resolution:** The research suggests play and deliberate practice are *complementary and sequential*, not opposed. In sports research, early "sampling years" of broad, playful exploration develop foundational intuitions and intrinsic motivation, while later "specialization years" of deliberate practice refine specific skills. Deliberate play correlates with quicker decision-making; deliberate practice correlates with better decision-making quality. The most effective development cycles between exploratory play and focused practice.

Applied to AI: play first to map the frontier (what can AI do? where does it fail?), then deliberate practice to develop specific capabilities (how to prompt for code review, how to structure analysis tasks, how to evaluate output quality). The play phase builds broad intuitions; the deliberate practice phase converts them into reliable, transferable skill. The sequence matters — deliberate practice without prior exploration means practicing in the wrong territory.

### 2.2 The "Play Can Reinforce Enchantment" Problem

**From the project's existing Part 1 research (the Talking Library):**
Play with AI can reinforce rather than break the enchantment. If a user "plays" by giving AI impressive-sounding prompts and marveling at impressive-sounding outputs, they are deepening the ELIZA effect, not developing skill. This is Sutton-Smith's ambiguity of play at work: play serves multiple functions, and not all of them are epistemic. Play-as-entertainment (delight in the output) is different from play-as-inquiry (testing limits, probing failures).

The Self-Automators in the BCG study could be seen as "playing" — they experimented with handing work to AI entirely. But this was play without judgment, play without the craftsman's resistance. The AI produced fluent output and the Self-Automators accepted it, never encountering the productive friction that Sennett identifies as the learning mechanism.

**Implication:** The thesis needs qualification. Not all play is epistemically productive. Productive play requires: (1) attention to failure and resistance, not just success; (2) comparison against independent standards (domain knowledge, known-good answers); (3) progressive difficulty, not repetition of comfortable tasks. These are exactly the conditions Ericsson identifies for deliberate practice — suggesting the boundary between productive play and deliberate practice is blurrier than it first appears.

### 2.3 Expertise Matters: You Can't Tinker Without Domain Knowledge

**Dell'Acqua, McFowland, Mollick et al., "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier" (Harvard Business School, 2023; Organization Science, 2025)**
The BCG study's most troubling finding: for tasks *outside* the frontier, AI-assisted consultants were 19 percentage points *less likely* to produce correct solutions. Using AI made them worse, not just not better. This wasn't random — AI produced confident, plausible-sounding wrong answers that consultants accepted because they lacked the domain expertise to detect the errors.

**Implication:** Tinkering without domain knowledge isn't play — it's fumbling. The bricoleur's toolkit includes prior expertise, not just prompting technique. A consultant who doesn't know enough about the task to evaluate AI output cannot learn from AI's mistakes because they can't identify them. Play as epistemology requires a knowledgeable player. This creates a bootstrap problem: you need domain knowledge to play productively with AI, but the claim is that play is how you develop AI knowledge. The resolution is that play develops *AI competence* (knowing what the tool can do), not *domain competence* (knowing the subject matter). Both are needed; they are developed differently.

## 3. Adjacent Work

Sources addressing related phenomena that inform the thesis without directly arguing for or against it.

### 3.1 Affordance Theory and Tool Discovery

**Gibson, *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception* (1979) — affordances**
Gibson coined "affordance" to describe what the environment offers an animal — the possibilities for action that exist in the relationship between agent and environment. Affordances are not properties of objects alone but relational: a chair affords sitting *for a human*, not for a fish. Affordances can be perceived directly without prior learning (a cliff affords falling) or may require discovery through exploration.

**Norman, *The Design of Everyday Things* (1988; revised 2013) — design affordances**
Norman adapted Gibson's concept for design: a well-designed object makes its affordances visible. A door handle affords pulling; a flat plate affords pushing. When affordances are hidden or misleading, users struggle.

**Gaver, "Technology Affordances" (CHI, 1991)**
Introduced the crucial distinction between *hidden affordances* (real capabilities not perceptually available to the user) and *false affordances* (perceived capabilities that don't match reality). AI tools exhibit both in abundance: hidden affordances (capabilities users never think to try, like using AI for systematic literature review or code refactoring) and false affordances (tasks the AI appears to handle but actually botches, like novel reasoning or precise calculation). Gaver's framework explains why AI interaction is so disorienting: the affordance landscape is simultaneously richer and more deceptive than users expect.

**Application to AI:** AI's affordances are radically hidden. A blank chat box reveals nothing about what the system can and cannot do. Unlike a hammer (which visibly affords striking) or even a spreadsheet (which visibly affords tabular calculation), AI presents no structural cues about its capabilities. The affordances are context-dependent (AI is good at summarizing *this* kind of text but not *that* kind), model-dependent (changes with each update), and prompt-dependent (the same task framed differently produces different results). This means affordance discovery must happen through exploration — through play. There is no alternative path to discovering what AI affords.

### 3.2 Situated Learning and Communities of Practice

**Lave & Wenger, *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation* (1991)**
Learning is not primarily an individual cognitive process but participation in communities of practice. Newcomers learn through "legitimate peripheral participation" — doing real but low-risk tasks alongside more experienced practitioners, gradually taking on more central roles. Knowledge is not transferred from expert to novice through instruction but constructed through participation.

**Wenger, *Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity* (1998)**
Extended the framework. Communities of practice share three elements: mutual engagement (doing things together), joint enterprise (shared understanding of what they're doing and why), and shared repertoire (accumulated resources — routines, vocabulary, tools, stories). Learning is becoming a different kind of person within the community, not just acquiring information.

**Application to AI:** The prompt engineering community, AI tinkerer forums, and practitioner blogs (Willison, Mollick, Appleton) function as communities of practice for AI tool use. They share a repertoire (prompt patterns, workflow structures, failure stories), engage in mutual practice (sharing experiments and results), and have a joint enterprise (figuring out how to use AI effectively). Lave & Wenger predict that the most effective AI learning will happen through participation in such communities — not through formal training. The Samba School metaphor from Papert converges: novices learn by watching and participating alongside practitioners who are further along the learning curve.

### 3.3 Design as Bricolage

**Vallgårda et al., "Interaction Design as a Bricolage Practice" (ACM Conference on Tangible, Embedded, and Embodied Interaction, 2015)**
Proposed bricolage as an interaction design methodology that promotes design qualities tuned to tangible and material computing. Bricolage is "a way of making which draws on what is already there both culturally and materially" and favors "making connections between the tools and materials at hand" over engineering from first principles. Establishes bricolage as a legitimate professional methodology, not just a personal learning style.

**Louridas, "Design as Bricolage: Anthropology Meets Design Thinking" (Design Studies, 1999)**
Argued that all design activity has an essential bricolage component — designers never start from scratch but always work with and recombine existing elements. The designer, like the bricoleur, is constrained by prior work and available materials, and creativity consists in novel recombination.

### 3.4 The METR Study and Confident Incompetence

**METR (Model Evaluation & Threat Research), "Are AI Assistants Making Us Less Productive?" (2025)**
Found that experienced open-source developers were 19% slower when using AI coding assistants, while simultaneously believing they were 20% faster. This is the most dramatic empirical demonstration of the explore-exploit problem: the developers had *not explored* enough to understand where AI helped and hurt, but their *exploitation* (using AI on real tasks) felt productive because the AI interface generated constant, fluent output.

The METR result is what you get when practitioners skip the play phase. Without sufficient tinkering to map the frontier, every use of AI is a gamble — but the fluent interface creates the subjective impression of competence. Play would have revealed the failure modes before they cost productivity.

## 4. Expanding Work

Sources that offer new dimensions, frameworks, or vocabularies for developing the thesis further.

### 4.1 The Cynefin Extensions: Safe-to-Fail Experiments

**Snowden, "The Origins of Cynefin" and subsequent work (2002-2024)**
Snowden's concept of "safe-to-fail experiments" in complex domains is the Cynefin framework's direct contribution to practice. In complex domains, you cannot predict outcomes, so you run multiple small experiments simultaneously, amplify those that succeed, and dampen those that fail. The experiments must be designed so that failure is informative rather than catastrophic.

**Application to AI:** Every AI interaction could be treated as a safe-to-fail experiment — a probe into the complex domain of AI capability. The key design principle is that failure should be cheap and visible. This means: don't deploy AI on high-stakes tasks before you've mapped the relevant frontier through low-stakes experimentation. The Cyborgs in the BCG study practiced this implicitly: continuous iterative dialogue is a series of small probes, each one cheap, each one informative.

### 4.2 Ecological Rationality

**Gigerenzer, Todd & the ABC Research Group, *Simple Heuristics That Make Us Smart* (1999); Gigerenzer, *Gut Feelings* (2007)**
Rationality is not about following optimal algorithms but about matching cognitive strategies to environmental structures — "ecological rationality." Simple heuristics that ignore information can outperform complex optimization when the environment is uncertain, variable, and has a particular statistical structure. The "less-is-more" effect: using fewer cues can improve prediction when the environment is noisy.

**Application to AI:** The bricoleur's approach to AI — using rough heuristics, gut feelings about when AI works, and pattern-matching from past experience — may be more ecologically rational than trying to develop systematic theories of AI capability. If the frontier is truly jagged and shifting, sophisticated models of AI capability may overfit to yesterday's model while simple heuristics ("AI is usually good at first drafts, usually bad at novel reasoning") may be more robust. Gigerenzer's framework legitimizes the tinkerer's intuition over the engineer's analysis.

### 4.3 Reflective Practice

**Schön, *The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action* (1983)**
Schön distinguishes between "technical rationality" (applying scientific theory to practice) and "reflection-in-action" (thinking while doing, adjusting in response to surprise). Professionals face situations of uncertainty, uniqueness, and value conflict that cannot be resolved by applying standard theories. They develop skill through "reflective conversation with the situation" — a process that is iterative, responsive to surprise, and essentially playful.

Schön's concept of the "reflective practicum" — a setting designed to support learning through doing — maps directly onto the idea of AI as playground. The practicum provides a transitional space between the classroom and the real world, where mistakes are expected and informative.

**Application to AI:** Schön's framework resolves a tension in the thesis. Play sounds unserious; reflection-in-action sounds professional. But they describe the same process: iterative engagement with an uncertain situation, adjusting in response to what happens. Schön gives the play thesis professional legitimacy. "Playing with AI" reframed as "reflective practice with AI" may be more palatable in organizational contexts while describing the same activity.

### 4.4 Knowledge in Complex Adaptive Systems

**Holland, *Hidden Order: How Adaptation Builds Complexity* (1995)**
Complex adaptive systems — economies, ecosystems, immune systems — produce emergent behaviors that cannot be predicted from component analysis. Agents in these systems learn through interaction and adaptation, not through understanding the system's structure. The parallel to AI: the model's behavior emerges from training in ways that resist analytical prediction. Users must learn through interaction.

**Kauffman, *At Home in the Universe: The Search for the Laws of Self-Organization and the Complexity of Evolution* (1995)**
Introduces the concept of a "fitness landscape" — a multi-dimensional space where neighboring positions may have radically different fitness values. The jagged frontier is a fitness landscape: seemingly similar tasks have radically different AI performance. Kauffman shows that on "rugged" fitness landscapes (many local optima, unpredictable peaks and valleys), exploratory search strategies outperform gradient-following (exploitation). This provides mathematical support for the play-first approach.

### 4.5 AI Literacy and Tinkering: 2020s Research

**Emerging AI literacy research (2023-2026)**
A growing body of work in learning sciences and HCI examines how people develop AI competence:

- A systematic review of 166 studies on AI-powered prompt engineering in education (2023-2025) found that hands-on activities play a crucial role in generative AI teaching. A "balanced educational approach blending automated tools with exploratory, project-based activities" is recommended.
- UNESCO (2023) reported that most higher education institutions lack formal curriculum frameworks for AI literacy, with students often developing competence informally through experimentation.
- Bibliometric analysis of AI literacy education research (2014-2024) shows explosive growth, with 2024 publications exceeding the total for 2023 by mid-year. The dominant recommendation across studies is design-based learning: brainstorming, hands-on exploratory practice, critical discussion, and reflection.
- The concept of "prompt literacy" has been defined as "a combination of knowledge, skills, and understanding necessary for engaging in dynamic and iterative interactions with generative AI" — a description that foregrounds the iterative, experimental nature of the competence.

These findings consistently point toward tinkering rather than instruction as the effective pedagogical mode for AI literacy — empirical support for the thesis, though the research is young and much of it is programmatic rather than rigorously tested.

**Nilsson, Ferholt & Lecusay, "Reconceptualizing the Relationship between Play and Learning" (Contemporary Issues in Early Childhood, 2018)**
Argue that play and learning are not sequential (play first, learn later) but constitutively intertwined: "many concepts and skills can only be learned through play." Meaning-making in play and meaning-making in exploration lead to development through the same mechanisms. This challenges the common assumption that play is a *preliminary* to serious learning — instead, play is a *mode* of learning that remains productive at all expertise levels.

## 5. Answers to Specific Questions

### Q1: Is there a sequence? Play first, then deliberate practice?

**Yes, the evidence converges on a sequence, though the boundary is blurry.**

Sports development research (Côté's Developmental Model of Sport Participation, multiple studies 2001-2020) consistently finds that early *diversification and play* ("sampling years") followed by later *specialization and deliberate practice* produces better long-term outcomes than early specialization alone. Athletes who play multiple sports and engage in unstructured play as children become better adult athletes than those who begin deliberate practice early.

Applied to AI: the optimal sequence is likely (1) exploratory play to map the frontier, develop intuitions, and build intrinsic motivation, followed by (2) deliberate practice to develop specific skills within the territories discovered through play. Organizations that skip to step 2 (training programs, best practices, prompt engineering courses) before allowing step 1 (unstructured experimentation) are repeating the early-specialization mistake.

The boundary is blurry because productive play already contains elements of deliberate practice (attention to results, adjustment based on feedback) and deliberate practice at its best retains elements of play (curiosity, willingness to try unusual approaches).

### Q2: Is AI a complex domain (Cynefin) or a complicated one?

**Complex, with pockets of complicated.**

The jagged frontier is the definitive marker of complexity: the relationship between input and output quality is emergent, not analytically predictable. No amount of expertise allows you to predict, for a novel task, whether AI will perform well — you must try and observe. This is the hallmark of Cynefin's complex domain.

However, *within* mapped territory, AI use can become complicated or even clear. Once you've discovered through play that AI reliably performs a specific task type, that specific use case moves from complex to complicated (you know the mechanism, you can replicate it). The frontier itself remains complex, but the interior becomes complicated. This means play is most valuable at the frontier — when you're doing something new with AI or encountering a new model — while established workflows can be refined through good practice and expertise.

### Q3: How does play connect to the three-part framework from the existing research?

**Play addresses all three parts simultaneously:**

1. **Play breaks the enchantment (Part 1 — The Talking Library):** Play-as-inquiry means seeking out failures, not just successes. When you discover that AI confidently generates plausible nonsense about your specific domain, the enchantment breaks. You see the tool for what it is — a compressed amalgam of knowledge that sometimes recombines accurately and sometimes doesn't. The ELIZA effect weakens through repeated exposure to failure. You stop experiencing AI as a knower and start experiencing it as a material with specific properties.

2. **Play maps the frontier (Part 2 — Taxonomy of Multiplication):** Every play session is an experiment that maps a small section of the jagged frontier. Where does AI multiply quality? Where does it multiply volume while degrading quality? Where does it help novices? Where does it mislead experts? These distinctions can only be discovered empirically, through tinkering. Play is the epistemology of frontier-mapping.

3. **Play exercises agency (Part 3 — Agency Is Upstream):** Play is inherently agentic — the player chooses what to try, evaluates results, decides what to try next. This is the opposite of the abdication pattern (Self-Automators) where users hand work to AI and accept whatever comes back. The tinkerer is always judging, always deciding, always exercising the agency that is upstream of effective AI use.

### Q4: Why does "playing with AI" feel like wasting time in professional contexts?

**Multiple reinforcing factors:**

- **March's exploitation bias:** Organizations systematically favor exploitation over exploration because exploitation produces more immediate, measurable returns. "Playing with AI" produces learning, but learning is diffuse and delayed. "Using AI to complete this task" produces a deliverable. Organizational incentives favor the deliverable.
- **Professional identity:** Knowledge workers are valued for competence, not exploration. Admitting you're "playing" with a tool implies you don't know how to use it, which threatens professional identity.
- **The productivity frame:** AI is marketed as a productivity tool. "Playing" implies non-productivity. But as March shows, organizations that only exploit become obsolete. The play phase is an investment in adaptability.
- **The visibility problem:** Play produces knowledge that is hard to measure and attribute. The insights gained from tinkering — this works, this doesn't, this is dangerous — are embodied in the practitioner, not captured in a report.
- **Schön's solution:** Reframing play as "reflective practice" may help. The same activity sounds professional when described as "structured experimentation to develop AI competence" rather than "playing around with ChatGPT."

## 6. Noted Gaps

### 6.1 The Productive Play Criteria

**No one has systematically defined what makes AI play productive vs. unproductive.** The thesis argues play is the right mode, but clearly some play builds skill (Cyborgs) and some play reinforces bad habits (Self-Automators) or produces the enchantment. The criteria that distinguish productive from unproductive play — likely involving attention to failure, domain knowledge, progressive difficulty, and critical evaluation — need to be specified. This is an original contribution opportunity.

### 6.2 Organizational Play

**The organizational conditions for productive AI play are undertheorized.** How do you create space for play in a professional context? What does an AI "samba school" look like inside a company? The Maker Movement provides some models (dedicated spaces, show-and-tell culture, low-stakes projects), but translation to AI and knowledge work is non-obvious. March's work predicts the organizational dynamics that will resist play, but offers less on how to overcome them.

### 6.3 Play Across Skill Levels

**How play functions differently for novices vs. experts with AI is unclear.** The BCG study suggests that novices benefit more from AI but also learn more from it, while experts are more likely to be harmed but potentially have deeper play (they can detect failures). Ericsson's framework suggests experts might need less play and more deliberate practice, while novices need more play and less structure. But this is speculative — no empirical work directly addresses the question.

### 6.4 The Longitudinal Gap

**No longitudinal studies track how AI tinkering develops into expertise over time.** We have cross-sectional data (the BCG study, the METR study) and practitioner narratives (Willison, Mollick), but no study follows learners from first encounter to competence, mapping the phases and transitions. This would be invaluable for testing the play-then-deliberate-practice sequence hypothesis.

### 6.5 Cultural Variation in Play

**The play thesis is framed within a Western, individualist tradition.** Papert's Samba School metaphor gestures toward collective play, and Lave & Wenger's communities of practice are explicitly social, but the dominant framing is individual-tinkerer-at-keyboard. How play with AI functions in collectivist cultures, in non-Western organizational contexts, and in oral/non-literate traditions remains unexplored.

### 6.6 Verification Needed

Several sources cited here draw on training knowledge through May 2025. The following should be verified:
- Specific 2025-2026 AI literacy studies cited in Section 4.5
