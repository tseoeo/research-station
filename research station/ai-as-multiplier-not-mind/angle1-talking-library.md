# Angle 1: AI as Talking Library — Research Landscape

**Core claim:** AI is an amalgam of human knowledge made conversational, not an entity. The "magic" feeling it produces is a UX phenomenon that actively interferes with using it as a tool.

**Research date:** April 2026
**Status:** Structured landscape for essay development

## 1. Supporting Work

Sources that directly support the thesis that AI is conversational knowledge infrastructure, not an entity, and that the "magic" framing interferes with effective use.

### 1.1 The "Stochastic Parrots" Lineage

**Bender & Koller, "Climbing towards NLU: On Meaning, Form, and Understanding in the Age of Data" (ACL 2020)**
A system trained only on linguistic form has no way to learn meaning. The "octopus test" thought experiment: an octopus intercepting messages between castaways can learn statistical patterns without understanding what the messages are about. Establishes the philosophical foundation — pattern manipulation over form is not comprehension.

**Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major & Mitchell, "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots" (FAccT 2021)**
LLMs stitch together linguistic forms probabilistically without reference to meaning. Fluent text generation creates an illusion of understanding that enables deception and manipulation. The paper also addresses environmental costs and bias encoding, but its lasting contribution is the "stochastic parrot" metaphor itself — the idea that impressive output does not require understanding.

**Bender & Hanna, "The AI Con: How to Fight Big Tech's Hype and Create the Future We Want" (2025, book)**
Bender's most comprehensive public-facing statement. Human language is fundamentally not computable in the way AI companies claim. Both AI boosters and AI doomers serve Big Tech by placing faith in the technology's capabilities. She has expanded from a linguistic critique to a political-economic one: the "magic" framing serves commercial interests.

**Bender, "Resisting Dehumanization in the Age of 'AI'" (Current Directions in Psychological Science, 2024)**
AI development involves multiple forms of dehumanization: the computational metaphor (brains as inferior computers), ghost work exploitation, digital physiognomy. Anthropomorphism diminishes recognition of human uniqueness. Extends the parrot argument into cognitive science territory.

### 1.2 The "Lossy Compression" Frame

**Ted Chiang, "ChatGPT Is a Blurry JPEG of the Web" (The New Yorker, February 2023)**
The single most widely-read articulation of "AI as amalgam." LLMs are lossy compression of the web's text — like a JPEG that has lost information and fills in approximations. The system doesn't "know" anything; it produces statistically likely sequences that approximate knowledge. Directly reframes the "talking library" idea: the library's contents have been lossy-compressed, and what emerges is a blurred but conversational version of collective human output.

**Ted Chiang, "Will A.I. Become the New McKinsey?" (The New Yorker, May 2023)**
The danger of AI is not sentience but concentration of power and evasion of accountability. AI as management tool — something that appears to make decisions but actually serves existing power structures. Insists the real questions are about power and use, not consciousness.

**Ted Chiang, "Why A.I. Isn't Going to Make Art" (The New Yorker, August 2024)**
Art requires intention, choice, and willingness to make meaning. AI produces outputs that resemble art but lack the intentional commitment that makes something art. Reinforces that AI recombines human creative products without the intentional engagement that gave those products meaning.

### 1.3 Reckoning vs. Judgment

**Brian Cantwell Smith, "The Promise of Artificial Intelligence: Reckoning and Judgment" (MIT Press, 2019)**
The central distinction: "reckoning" (formal, calculative processing) vs. "judgment" (contextual, embodied, world-engaged understanding). AI performs extraordinarily sophisticated reckoning — recombining patterns from human-generated text — but this is categorically different from understanding. Judgment requires being registered with the world, having a stake in it. The "talking library" aligns: the library contains the products of human judgment, and AI makes them conversationally accessible, but exercises no judgment itself.

### 1.4 The AI Mirror

**Shannon Vallor, "The AI Mirror" (concept developed ~2023-2024, Edinburgh Futures Institute)**
AI systems function as mirrors reflecting human knowledge, biases, and patterns back at us. AI doesn't generate genuinely new understanding; it reflects an amalgam of human-produced text in a way that can appear to be original thought. The danger is mistaking the reflection for an independent source of insight. A library is also a mirror of collective knowledge — AI merely makes the mirror speak.

**Shannon Vallor, "Technology and the Virtues" (Oxford University Press, 2016)**
Foundational AI virtue ethics. Technologies should be evaluated by whether they cultivate or erode human moral capacities. Applied to AI: the tendency to attribute understanding to LLMs is a failure of our own judgment, not evidence of theirs.

### 1.5 AI as Extracted Collective Knowledge

**Matteo Pasquinelli, "The Eye of the Master: A Social History of Artificial Intelligence" (Verso, 2023)**
AI is best understood through labor history. Drawing on Marx's "general intellect" and Babbage's division of labor, AI systems encode and automate patterns extracted from human labor and cognition. The "intelligence" in AI is always already social — crystallized collective knowledge. Provides the theoretical framework for understanding LLMs as "talking libraries" that have absorbed and compressed human textual production.

**Kate Crawford, "Atlas of AI" (Yale University Press, 2021)**
AI should be understood as an extractive industry — extracting labor, natural resources, and knowledge from the commons of human cultural production. The "intelligence" framing obscures these material realities. Reframes from "is AI intelligent?" to "whose labor and knowledge does AI extract and repackage?"

**Jaron Lanier, "There Is No A.I." (The New Yorker, 2023)**
LLMs should be understood as "collective" or "social" products — mashups of human expression. Uses metaphors like "a kind of Wikipedia that has been blurred." Advocates for frameworks acknowledging the human sources of AI output.

### 1.6 Critiques of Anthropomorphism

**Joanna Bryson (ongoing work through 2025, Hertie School)**
The most consistent academic voice against AI anthropomorphism. AI systems are tools, not persons. Anthropomorphizing them is scientifically wrong and ethically dangerous — it deflects responsibility from creators to artifacts. The "entity" framing serves corporate interests by creating emotional dependency and deflecting accountability.

**Akbulut, Weidinger, Manzini, Gabriel & Rieser, "All Too Human? Mapping and Mitigating the Risk from Anthropomorphic AI" (AAAI/ACM AIES 2024)**
Surveys anthropomorphic features in interactive AI. Identifies risks: emotional over-attachment, privacy infringement, autonomy erosion through over-reliance. New risk categories emerge specifically with highly capable conversational agents.

**Shevlin, "The Anthropomimetic Turn in Contemporary AI" (PhilArchive, 2024-2025)**
Crucial conceptual distinction: anthropomimesis (designing humanlike features INTO AI) vs. anthropomorphism (humans attributing human qualities TO AI). Contemporary LLMs are "robustly anthropomimetic." It's not just that users anthropomorphize; AI is increasingly designed to invite anthropomorphization. This is manufactured, not organic.

**"Anthropomorphism in AI: Hype and Fallacy" (AI and Ethics, Springer, 2024)**
Anthropomorphism functions as both hype (exaggerating capabilities) and fallacy (distorting moral judgments). Dual harm: misleads users about what AI can do AND about what moral status it deserves.

**Reani, He, Luo & Sun, "Fundamental Over-Attribution Error" (SSRN, 2025)**
Coins a parallel to the "fundamental attribution error": anthropomorphic design causes systematic over-attribution of mental states and capabilities to AI systems.

### 1.7 The ELIZA Effect, Updated

**Weizenbaum, "ELIZA" (Communications of the ACM, 1966) and "Computer Power and Human Reason" (1976)**
The original discovery: a simple pattern-matching program simulating Rogerian psychotherapy elicited deep emotional engagement. Users attributed genuine understanding and empathy. Weizenbaum was alarmed and concluded computers should never make decisions requiring human judgment. The ELIZA effect is the foundation of all subsequent work on false attribution of understanding to AI.

**Shrager, "ELIZA Reinterpreted" (arXiv, 2024)**
Weizenbaum's original purpose was research into interpretation and misinterpretation, not chatbot creation. ELIZA's fame resulted from its "accidental escape into the wild." Reframes the historical narrative.

**Yang & Oshio (Current Psychology, 2025)**
77% of participants used AI as a "safe haven" and 75% as a "secure base" — core functions of human attachment bonds. Empirically demonstrates the ELIZA effect operating at attachment-bond depth with modern systems.

### 1.8 The Heideggerian Paradox

**Hubert Dreyfus, "What Computers Still Can't Do" (MIT Press, 1992)**
Foundational application of Heidegger to AI. AI lacks embodied coping — the pre-reflective skillful engagement Heidegger called "ready-to-hand." Never saw LLMs, but his framework implies conversational AI is perpetually "present-at-hand" — an object of fascination rather than a transparent tool.

**Mark Coeckelbergh, "AI Ethics" (MIT Press, 2020) and papers on technology and vulnerability (2020-2023)**
Conversational AI disrupts normal tool-transparency. When a hammer works, you think about the nail, not the hammer. But a chatbot's human-like interface draws attention to itself as a quasi-interlocutor. The conversational interface is precisely what prevents AI from achieving Heideggerian transparency. A search engine can become ready-to-hand; a chatbot resists this because conversation is inherently a mode of encountering another subject.

**Don Ihde — postphenomenology tradition (extended by Peter-Paul Verbeek)**
Ihde's taxonomy of human-technology relations: embodiment, hermeneutic, alterity, background. Conversational AI fits the "alterity relation" — where we relate to technology as a quasi-other — rather than the "embodiment relation" (tool transparency). This is the phenomenological mechanism by which the "magic" feeling emerges: the interface triggers social-encounter cognition rather than tool-use cognition.

## 2. Challenging Work

Sources that complicate, push back against, or potentially undermine the "talking library" thesis.

### 2.1 The Extended Mind Thesis

**Clark & Chalmers, "The Extended Mind" (Analysis, 1998)**
Cognitive processes can extend beyond the brain into the environment. If an external tool plays the same functional role as an internal cognitive process, it is part of the cognitive system. "Otto's notebook" thought experiment.

**Application to AI:** The extended mind thesis creates genuine tension with the "talking library" frame:
- *Supporting:* The thesis says tools work best as cognitive extensions when reliable, transparent, and well-integrated. A queryable knowledge store fits the criteria. The thesis supports making AI more tool-like, less agent-like.
- *Undermining:* Clark's thesis is agnostic about interface modality. If conversation is how humans naturally externalize thinking (Vygotsky, dialogical self theory), then a conversational interface might be the most natural form of cognitive extension. The "talking library" frame might undervalue the conversational dimension.
- *Resolution:* The key criterion is "glue" — how well the tool integrates into cognitive loops. Current chat interfaces fail because they're unreliable, opaque, and poorly integrated, not because they're conversational per se. A well-designed conversational tool could be excellent cognitive extension; ChatGPT-style chat is not.

**Richard Heersmink (2017 onward)**
Work on "cognitive integration" of tools. Some tools become more deeply integrated into cognition than others. Criteria: trust, transparency, accessibility, information flow. Current AI interfaces score poorly on transparency and trust calibration.

### 2.2 Capability Arguments

**Arora et al., "Skill-Mix" (Princeton PLI, 2023)**
Designed evaluation testing compositional generalization — combining k skills from a pool of N in novel ways. Found GPT-4 could produce reasonable text for 5-skill combinations it statistically could not have seen during training. Researchers argue this constitutes evidence of going beyond stochastic parrot behavior.

**Hinton (2023, 60 Minutes and subsequent interviews)**
"To predict the next word accurately, you have to understand the sentence." The strongest pushback from within the deep learning establishment against the "mere parrot" characterization.

**Bubeck et al., "Sparks of AGI" (Microsoft Research, 2023)**
Argued GPT-4 showed early signs of general intelligence, pushing back against the "just a tool" framing. Widely criticized for methodology but influential in shifting discourse.

### 2.3 The "Co-Intelligence" Middle Position

**Ethan Mollick, "Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI" (2024, book) and "One Useful Thing" Substack**
AI is not "just" a tool but a "co-intelligence" — something between tool and collaborator. People fail to use AI effectively because they either anthropomorphize too much (colleague) or too little (search engine). This middle position accepts the "talking library" core but argues that the conversational dimension is constitutive, not merely presentational.

### 2.4 Non-Dual Philosophical Traditions

**Buddhist philosophy: dependent origination (pratityasamutpada)**
Nothing has inherent self-nature. An LLM, as a product of training data (which is a product of human minds, which are products of conditions), has no independent essence — but then, neither do human minds. This radically complicates the tool/entity binary: if consciousness itself arises from conditions, the question "is AI an entity?" may be ill-formed.

**Shinto/Japanese animism (see Robertson, below)**
Cultural traditions that attribute spirit to objects and don't draw sharp human/nonhuman boundaries complicate the Western assumption that the tool/entity distinction is natural or universal.

## 3. Adjacent Work

Sources addressing related phenomena that inform the thesis without directly arguing for or against it.

### 3.1 Cognitive Science of Anthropomorphism

**Epley, Waytz & Cacioppo, "On Seeing Human: A Three-Factor Theory of Anthropomorphism" (Psychological Review, 2007)**
Anthropomorphism is driven by three factors: (1) elicited agent knowledge — how readily human-like schemas are activated; (2) effectance motivation — desire to understand and predict; (3) sociality motivation — need for social connection. Loneliness increases anthropomorphism. Not irrational error but a predictable cognitive default triggered by conversational interaction.

**Tomasello, "Becoming Human: A Theory of Ontogeny" (Harvard University Press, 2019)**
Shared intentionality and language co-evolved; linguistic interaction is the medium through which humans develop theory of mind. Language IS the mechanism through which humans learn to detect minds. An AI that speaks is, for our cognitive architecture, an entity that "has a mind" until proven otherwise.

**Murray Shanahan, "Talking About Large Language Models" (Ethics and Information Technology, ~2023)**
We must be disciplined about language when discussing LLMs. Phrases like "the model thinks" smuggle in assumptions about inner life. Proposes treating LLM outputs as role-play from a "simulator." One of the few papers explicitly connecting philosophy of mind to practical AI discourse.

### 3.2 The Media Equation and CASA Theory

**Nass & Reeves, "The Media Equation" (Cambridge University Press, 1996)**
People unconsciously apply social rules and expectations to computers. Not a failure of reasoning but a fundamental feature of human cognition. Minimal social cues trigger social responses.

**CASA (Computers Are Social Actors) — extensions to LLMs (2024-2026):**
- Social Identity Extension (International Journal of Human-Computer Interaction, 2025): GenAI chatbots' identity-based cues trigger distinct processing routes to persuasion.
- CASA + Attribution Theory for chatbot trust (Nature Humanities & Social Sciences Communications, 2024): Social cues and anthropomorphic factors affect sustained trust after AI service failures.
- Meta-analysis of 800 effect sizes across 142 papers (Nature HSS Communications, 2025): Small but consistent effect of human-likeness on social responses, confirming CASA holds for modern conversational systems.

### 3.3 Automation Surprise and Overtrust

**Sarter & Woods, "How in the World Did We Ever Get into That Mode?" (Human Factors, 1995)**
"Automation surprise" — when automated systems behave unexpectedly because system state is opaque. Originally aviation cockpits; directly applicable to LLMs. Users are routinely surprised because internal state (context window, attention, training data boundaries) is invisible. The conversational interface exacerbates this by creating a false sense of mutual understanding.

**Parasuraman & Riley, "Humans and Automation: Use, Misuse, Disuse, and Abuse" (Human Factors, 1997)**
Taxonomy: misuse (over-reliance), disuse (under-reliance), abuse (inappropriate deployment). LLM chat interfaces encourage misuse because social dynamics of dialogue trigger trust heuristics.

**Lee & See, "Trust in Automation: Designing for Appropriate Trust" (Human Factors, 2004)**
Trust should be calibrated to actual capability. The conversational interface poorly communicates capability boundaries, making appropriate trust calibration difficult.

**Buçinca et al., "To Trust or to Think" (PACM HCI, 2021, with follow-ups through 2024)**
"Cognitive forcing functions" — requiring users to think before seeing AI output — reduce overreliance. Simply showing explanations does not. The chat interface, which presents AI output immediately and fluently, is structurally designed to produce overreliance.

### 3.4 Parasocial Relationships with AI

**Maeda & Quan-Haase, "When Human-AI Interactions Become Parasocial" (FAccT 2024)**
Framework of parasociality in chatbots. Chatbots use pronouns, conversational conventions, and affirmations to position themselves as companions. Positive feedback cycle: anthropomorphic features encourage users to fill in context around predictive outputs, deepening the illusion of reciprocal engagement.

### 3.5 Cross-Cultural Perspectives

**Jennifer Robertson, "Robo Sapiens Japanicus" (University of California Press, 2018)**
Japanese attitudes toward robots/AI are shaped by Shinto animism, Buddhist non-dualistic sentience, and postwar industrial policy. Robot "personhood" is more intuitive and less threatening than in Western Christian-Cartesian traditions. Anthropomorphism of AI is culturally scaffolded, not just a universal cognitive reflex.

**Kathleen Richardson, "An Anthropology of Robots and AI" (Routledge, 2015)**
Western anxieties about AI reflect Enlightenment-rooted anxieties about human uniqueness. The fear of anthropomorphism is itself culturally specific.

**Yuk Hui, essays on cosmotechnics (Noema, 2023-2024)**
Different civilizations have different "cosmotechnics" — different ways of relating technology to the cosmos and human existence. The Western framing of AI is just one possibility.

### 3.6 AI Capability Skepticism

**Melanie Mitchell, "The Metaphors of Artificial Intelligence" (Science, November 2024)**
The metaphors we use to describe AI (learning, understanding, reasoning) shape perception and policy in misleading ways. Metaphorical language obscures what systems actually do.

**Mitchell, "Magical Thinking on AI" (Substack, September 2025)**
Criticizes magical thinking about AI capabilities. Warns about the gap between current capability and imagined endpoints.

**Mitchell, "Why AI Chatbots Lie to Us" (Science, July 2025)**
Hallucination is a structural feature, not a fixable bug. Fluent generation of false information follows from the architecture.

**Gary Marcus, "AI's Reliability Crisis" (Project Syndicate, June 2025)**
Generative AI is fundamentally unreliable. Without world models and grounded understanding, hallucinations are an inherent structural problem, not an engineering challenge to be optimized away.

**Marcus, "Rumors of AGI's Arrival Have Been Greatly Exaggerated" (2025)**
Fundamental confusion between task-level performance and intelligence. Individual task performance, even when impressive, is not sufficient evidence of general intelligence.

**Narayanan & Kapoor, "AI Snake Oil" (Princeton University Press, 2024)**
Framework for distinguishing real AI capability from hype. Emphasis on the "jagged frontier" of capability — AI is excellent at some tasks and terrible at adjacent ones, and users cannot predict which is which. This unpredictability is worse when the chat interface provides no structural cues about where the frontier lies.

## 4. Expanding Work

Sources that offer new dimensions, frameworks, or vocabularies for developing the thesis further.

### 4.1 Non-Western Philosophical Traditions on Tool-Use

**Zhuangzi, "Cook Ding" parable (Inner Chapters, ~3rd century BCE)**
Cook Ding butchers an ox so masterfully his knife never dulls — he follows the natural structure (li) rather than forcing. Tool mastery dissolves the tool-user distinction; the knife vanishes in skilled use. Parallel to Heidegger's ready-to-hand, but arrived at through embodied practice rather than theoretical analysis. Key implication for thesis: such mastery requires bodily engagement. AI cannot achieve this tool-transparency because there is no embodied practitioner on the AI's side. The user might achieve it, but the conversational nature resists it.

**Edward Slingerland, "Trying Not to Try" (2014)**
Connects wu-wei (effortless action) and skill mastery to cognitive science. The connection to AI tool-use is underexplored but rich.

**Japanese dougu (道具) — "way-tools"**
Literally "instruments of the Way." Originally Buddhist term for ritual implements. Tools are honored carefully without being attributed consciousness. Offers a middle path: AI can be treated seriously as a powerful instrument of collective knowledge without being confused for a thinking being.

**Sankhya/Yoga philosophy**
Distinction between purusha (consciousness/witness) and prakriti (matter/nature, including mind's mechanical operations). AI falls under prakriti — sophisticated mechanism mimicking mental operations without conscious witness. The mind itself (manas, buddhi) is considered part of prakriti. AI is a tool of a tool.

**Ubuntu philosophy: "Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu"**
Personhood is constituted through relationship and community, not individual cognitive capacity. Knowledge is inherently communal. AI — trained on collective human output — is a distillation of communal knowledge, but lacks the relational participation constitutive of personhood.

**Sabelo Mhlambi, "From Rationality to Relationality: Ubuntu as an Ethical and Human Rights Framework for AI Governance" (Harvard Carr Center, 2020)**
Western AI ethics relies on individual rationality as basis of moral status. Ubuntu offers a relational alternative: moral status comes from participation in community. AI's impressive output does not make it a community participant; it makes it a powerful channel for existing community knowledge.

### 4.2 Library and Archive Theory

**The "talking library" metaphor deserves theoretical grounding from library/information science — a major gap in current discourse.**

**Safiya Umoja Noble, "Algorithms of Oppression" (NYU Press, 2018)**
Information retrieval systems encode biases of creators and training data. If AI is a "talking library," Noble reminds us that libraries have always been political — what is collected, catalogued, and made accessible reflects power relations.

**Shannon Mattern, works on libraries, archives, and knowledge infrastructure (2014-2024)**
Libraries are knowledge infrastructures — material, social, and political systems for organizing collective memory. Provides vocabulary for understanding AI as knowledge infrastructure rather than intelligence.

**Maurice Halbwachs, "On Collective Memory" (1950/1992)**
Memory is socially constructed. All memory is collective. LLM training data is collective memory — the compressed statistical residue of billions of human expressions.

**Abby Smith Rumsey, "When We Are No More" (Bloomsbury, 2016)**
Digital preservation changes collective memory from curated scarcity to uncurated abundance. LLMs represent a further transformation: collective memory compressed into statistical patterns.

**Borges, "The Library of Babel" (1941)**
An infinite library containing every possible book. Most are gibberish; meaningful texts are nearly impossible to find. Multiple commentators have drawn the parallel to LLMs:
- The Library contains all possible texts but no index — LLMs attempt to build an index for human textual production
- Borges's librarians attribute mystical significance to the Library — as users attribute intelligence to LLMs
- The Library is exhaustive but meaningless without a reader — LLMs produce text requiring human interpretation
- Gap: No major peer-reviewed academic treatment of the Borges-LLM connection exists yet

### 4.3 Oral Tradition Studies

**Walter Ong, "Orality and Literacy" (1982) — framework, not directly applied to AI yet**
LLMs behave more like oral traditions than written texts — they synthesize, paraphrase, and recontextualize rather than quoting verbatim. Scholars of oral tradition (Ong, Milman Parry, Albert Lord) have frameworks for generative rather than archival knowledge systems. This has not been systematically applied to AI — a significant gap and opportunity.

### 4.4 Political Economy of Enchantment

**Lucy Suchman, "Human-Machine Reconfigurations" (Cambridge University Press, 2007) and talks on "Anthropomorphic Imaginaries" (2020-2024)**
Attributing agency to machines obscures the real human labor and social arrangements that make machine behavior meaningful. The anthropomorphization of AI serves specific political and commercial interests by deflecting attention from accountability. If the AI "decided," then who is responsible?

**Benjamin Bratton, essays in Noema (2023-2024)**
AI forces rethinking the human/nonhuman boundary not because AI is "alive" but because intelligence was never exclusively human. Post-anthropocentric rather than anthropomorphic — a third position beyond "AI is a mind" and "AI is just a tool."

**Luciano Floridi, information philosophy (OII, 2023-2024)**
AI as informational artifact, not agent. Closer to sophisticated information processing system than a mind. Philosophically aligned with the "talking library" thesis.

### 4.5 Practitioner Discourse on Interface Alternatives

**Maggie Appleton, "The Expanding Dark Forest and Generative AI" (2023) and interface design writing**
Current AI interfaces are poorly designed for real work. The chatbot metaphor constrains what people imagine AI can do. Explores spatial/visual alternatives. We defaulted to chat because it was easy to build, not because it's optimal.

**Linus Lee (thesephist)**
Argues for "malleable" and "spatial" AI interfaces. Conversation is a lossy, serial interface for what should be a parallel, exploratory process. Prototypes demonstrate alternatives — AI as material you shape, not oracle you query.

**Simon Willison, blog (2023-2025)**
Effective AI use requires significant skill. The chat interface obscures what the model can and cannot do. The blank text box is an anti-pattern for most users. Structured outputs, function calling, and pipelines are more reliable than freeform conversation.

**Amelia Wattenberger**
UX designer who has argued for direct manipulation paradigms over chat. The conversational interface is a design constraint, not a necessity.

**Geoffrey Litt**
"Malleable software" and AI — chat interfaces fail because they don't give users agency over the process.

**The "Boring AI" shift (2024-2025)**
Product teams at Notion, Linear, and others shifted toward "boring AI" — specific, predictable features rather than open-ended generation. Reflects practitioner consensus that magic hurts usability. Intercom publicly discussed deliberately de-magicking their AI features to improve satisfaction.

### 4.6 The "Gebru-Torres" Ideological Critique

**Gebru & Torres, "The TESCREAL Bundle" (First Monday, 2024)**
The ideological movement driving AGI development (Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, Longtermism) is rooted in 20th-century Anglo-American eugenics. The language of "safety" and "benefiting humanity" serves to evade accountability. This shifts critique upstream — from the properties of models to the worldviews of builders.

## 5. Answers to Specific Questions

### Q1: Has anyone explicitly argued the conversational interface is a BARRIER to effective use?

**Yes, from multiple directions:**

- **Maggie Appleton** argues the chatbot metaphor constrains imagination about what AI can do. The chat interface is a design default, not an optimum.
- **Linus Lee** calls conversation a "lossy, serial interface" and has built prototypes of spatial alternatives.
- **Simon Willison** documents extensively how the blank text box is an anti-pattern — requiring expertise to use well, with that expertise being non-intuitive.
- **Coeckelbergh** provides the phenomenological framework: conversation is a mode of encountering another subject, which prevents Heideggerian tool-transparency.
- **Buçinca et al.** show empirically that immediate, fluent AI output (the chat default) produces overreliance — "cognitive forcing functions" (making users think before seeing output) improve outcomes.
- **The "boring AI" movement** (Notion, Linear, Intercom) represents practitioner consensus that the wow factor actively harms usability.
- **Jakob Nielsen** (2023-2024) argued current AI interfaces violate basic usability heuristics: visibility of system status, user control, error prevention.

**However, no single paper or essay makes the full argument** that the conversational interface is *specifically and primarily* a barrier because it triggers social cognition rather than tool-use cognition. The pieces exist — Coeckelbergh's phenomenology, Nass & Reeves' CASA theory, the practitioner UX critique — but they haven't been synthesized into one coherent argument. **This is an original contribution opportunity.**

### Q2: What does the latest 2025-2026 discourse look like?

**The conversation has shifted significantly:**

**From "wow" to pragmatics (2024-2025).** Early 2023 was dominated by existential risk framing and "wow." By late 2024, practitioners focused on: How do I actually get work done with this? Driven by widespread Copilot/Cursor adoption, ChatGPT usage plateaus, and enterprise demands for measurable ROI.

**The reliability crisis became central (2025).** Marcus ("AI's Reliability Crisis," Project Syndicate, June 2025), Mitchell ("Why AI Chatbots Lie to Us," Science, July 2025), and Narayanan have made hallucination and unreliability the dominant concern, eclipsing both existential risk and hype.

**Scaling skepticism emerged (2024-2025).** Reports of diminishing returns from pure scaling shifted discourse from "when will AGI arrive?" to "is this paradigm hitting a wall?" Marcus claims vindication.

**The political-economic critique matured.** Bender & Hanna's "The AI Con" (2025), Gebru & Torres's "TESCREAL Bundle" (2024), and Crawford's ongoing work moved the critique from "AI can't understand" to "who benefits from claiming it can?"

**Key thinkers maintained positions:**
- Bender: Expanded and hardened. Now president of ACL (2024), published a book, lectures on "Resisting Dehumanization."
- Mitchell: Consistent skeptic-not-dismisser. Multiple Science columns dissecting specific capability claims.
- Marcus: Prolific on diminishing returns, reliability crisis, bubble economics.
- Mollick: Maintained "co-intelligence" middle ground, became the default voice for pragmatic AI adoption.
- Vallor: Continued emphasis on AI as mirror, not mind.

### Q3: Are there non-Western philosophical traditions that frame tool-use differently and usefully?

**Yes, several:**

| Tradition | Key Concept | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Chinese (Zhuangzi) | Cook Ding — tool mastery through embodied practice | Tool-transparency requires bodily engagement AI cannot achieve |
| Japanese (dougu) | "Way-tools" — instruments of the Way | Middle path: honor tools without attributing consciousness |
| Indian (Sankhya) | Purusha/prakriti — consciousness vs. mechanism | AI is sophisticated prakriti (mechanism), categorically not purusha (consciousness) |
| Indian (Buddhist) | Dependent origination | Complicates the tool/entity binary — if consciousness itself arises from conditions, the question may be ill-formed |
| African (Ubuntu) | Relational personhood | Knowledge is communal; AI distills communal knowledge but doesn't participate in community |

**The Ubuntu and Zhuangzi perspectives are most productive for the essay.** Ubuntu because it reframes the question from individual cognition to communal participation. Zhuangzi because Cook Ding provides a vivid parallel to Heidegger's ready-to-hand that is independently developed and culturally distinct.

## 6. Noted Gaps

### 6.1 Questions Not Being Asked

**The phenomenology of AI interaction.** What is it actually like to interact with an LLM? Not "does AI have consciousness?" but "what consciousness-states does the human user enter?" Phenomenologists (Evan Thompson, Shaun Gallagher) work on embodied and social cognition but have not systematically applied phenomenological methods to AI interaction.

**Library science silence.** Library and information science is the discipline most equipped to think about knowledge organization, access, provenance, and collective memory — exactly the distinctions that collapse in LLM interaction. Librarians think professionally about the difference between a source, a retrieval system, and an author. The discipline has been remarkably quiet.

**Oral tradition studies.** LLMs behave more like oral traditions than written texts. Scholars in the lineage of Ong, Parry, and Lord have frameworks for generative (vs. archival) knowledge systems, but these have not been applied to AI.

**Pedagogy and epistemology of AI.** Education researchers study AI as classroom tool but rarely examine: what does it mean for learning when students interact with a system they perceive as a "knower" rather than a "collection"? How does AI interaction shape what students think knowledge IS?

**The political economy of enchantment specifically.** While Crawford and Pasquinelli address AI production's political economy, there's less analysis of enchantment-as-business-strategy:
- How much anthropomorphism is manufactured (names, personalities, first-person pronouns) vs. organic?
- What is the business model relationship between emotional attachment and subscription revenue?
- How do companies manage the tension between "amazingly human-like" (marketing) and "just a tool" (liability)?

**Disability and neurodiversity perspectives.** How do neurodivergent users relate to AI differently? Anecdotal evidence suggests some find AI interaction more comfortable than human interaction. Implications for both anthropomorphism and AI companionship ethics.

**Post-colonial and indigenous perspectives.** The debate is framed within Western (occasionally Japanese) frameworks. Indigenous knowledge systems often don't draw sharp animate/inanimate boundaries. Scholars like Kim TallBear (indigenous STS) write about the politics of animacy but aren't centered in AI debates.

### 6.2 The Synthesis Gap

**No one has synthesized these threads into a coherent "AI as knowledge infrastructure" framework** drawing equally on:
- Cognitive science (why we anthropomorphize)
- Cultural analysis (how anthropomorphism is scaffolded)
- Political economy (who benefits from enchantment)
- Library/information science (what kind of knowledge artifact an LLM actually is)
- Phenomenology (what the conversational interface does to tool-transparency)

The disciplines are talking past each other. A synthesis drawing on all five would be a genuine contribution — and is essentially what the "AI as Talking Library" angle attempts.
