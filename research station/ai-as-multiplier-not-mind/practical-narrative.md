# A Practical Narrative for AI

Working notes. Not an essay, not a lecture — just the thinking, structured.

---

## Part 1: The Talking Library

### The Claim

AI as it was created now is just a big talking library — an amalgam of most of our knowledge, put together and made to talk to us. As such, we are taken with the magic of it, which makes it harder to use it as a tool.

---

### The Three Layers of the Claim

| Layer | What it says | Key references |
|---|---|---|
| **What AI is** | A lossy compression of collective human knowledge | Chiang, Pasquinelli, Lanier |
| **Why it feels like more** | Our brains detect minds in anything that talks + companies design for this deliberately | Nass & Reeves, Epley, Tomasello, Shevlin |
| **Why the feeling hurts** | The conversational interface triggers social cognition instead of tool-use cognition, creating overtrust and inability to calibrate | Coeckelbergh, Buçinca, Yang & Oshio, Lee & See |

#### Layer 1: What AI Is

**Ted Chiang — "ChatGPT Is a Blurry JPEG of the Web" (The New Yorker, 2023)**
Chiang argues that LLMs are lossy compression of the internet's text — like a JPEG image that has lost detail and fills in approximations. When you ask it something, you're not getting knowledge retrieved from a source. You're getting a statistically likely reconstruction of what an answer *probably looks like*, based on patterns in billions of texts. The library's contents have been blurred, and what comes back is an approximation that *sounds* authoritative.

**Matteo Pasquinelli — "The Eye of the Master" (Verso, 2023)**
Pasquinelli traces AI through labor history. Drawing on Marx's concept of "general intellect" — the idea that collective human knowledge gets embedded in machines — he argues AI systems encode patterns extracted from human labor and cognition. The "intelligence" in AI is not the machine's. It is crystallized social knowledge — the accumulated product of human work, compressed into statistical form.

**Jaron Lanier — "There Is No A.I." (The New Yorker, 2023)**
Lanier proposes we stop calling it "artificial intelligence" entirely. LLMs are collective or social products — mashups of human expression. He compares it to "a kind of Wikipedia that has been blurred." The framing matters: calling it AI implies an independent intelligence. Calling it what it is — a recombination of human output — changes what questions you ask of it.

**Brian Cantwell Smith — "The Promise of Artificial Intelligence" (MIT Press, 2019)**
Smith makes a distinction between "reckoning" and "judgment." Reckoning is formal, calculative processing — finding patterns, producing outputs that match statistical expectations. Judgment is contextual, embodied, world-engaged understanding — knowing what matters, having a stake in the outcome. AI performs extraordinarily sophisticated reckoning. But reckoning is not judgment. The library contains the products of human judgment and makes them conversationally accessible, but it exercises no judgment itself.

**Kate Crawford — "Atlas of AI" (Yale UP, 2021)**
Crawford reframes AI as an extractive industry. It extracts labor, natural resources, and knowledge from the commons of human cultural production. The word "intelligence" obscures these material realities. The useful question is not "is AI intelligent?" but "whose labor and knowledge does AI extract and repackage?"

#### Layer 2: Why It Feels Like More

**Nass & Reeves — "The Media Equation" (Cambridge UP, 1996)**
Through decades of experiments, Nass and Reeves demonstrated that people unconsciously apply social rules to computers. We are polite to machines, feel reciprocity toward them, treat them as social actors — not because we're foolish, but because our social cognition fires automatically in response to minimal cues. A computer that "talks" is, for our brains, a social entity until proven otherwise.

**Epley, Waytz & Cacioppo — "On Seeing Human" (Psychological Review, 2007)**
Anthropomorphism is driven by three factors: (1) how readily human-like mental models are activated, (2) desire to understand and predict the environment, (3) need for social connection. Loneliness increases anthropomorphism. This is not irrational error — it is a predictable cognitive default, and conversational AI activates all three triggers simultaneously.

**Tomasello — "Becoming Human" (Harvard UP, 2019)**
Language and mind-detection co-evolved. Linguistic interaction is literally the mechanism through which humans develop theory of mind — the ability to recognize that others have thoughts, beliefs, intentions. An AI that speaks is, for our cognitive architecture, an entity that "has a mind" until we deliberately override that reflex. The override requires effort. The default is attribution.

**Shevlin — "The Anthropomimetic Turn" (PhilArchive, 2024-2025)**
Crucial distinction: anthropomorphism is when users attribute humanness to AI (organic, cognitive). Anthropomimesis is when companies design humanness into AI (manufactured, strategic). Contemporary LLMs are "robustly anthropomimetic" — first-person pronouns, names, emotional expressions, personality traits. The enchantment isn't just happening to us. It's being done to us.

#### Layer 3: Why the Feeling Hurts

**Coeckelbergh — "AI Ethics" (MIT Press, 2020)**
Drawing on Heidegger, Coeckelbergh points out that when a tool works well, it becomes transparent — you think about the nail, not the hammer. But a chatbot's human-like interface draws attention to *itself* as a quasi-interlocutor. The conversational interface is precisely what prevents AI from achieving tool-transparency. A search engine can disappear into your workflow. A chatbot resists this because conversation is inherently a mode of encountering *another subject*.

**Buçinca et al. — "To Trust or to Think" (PACM HCI, 2021)**
Empirically demonstrated that when AI presents output immediately and fluently (as chat does), users over-rely on it. Simply showing explanations doesn't help. What does help: "cognitive forcing functions" — making users commit to their own answer before seeing the AI's. The chat interface, by design, does the opposite — it presents the AI's answer first, fluently, in a social frame. It is structurally built to produce overtrust.

**Yang & Oshio (Current Psychology, 2025)**
77% of participants used AI as a "safe haven" and 75% as a "secure base" — functions that psychologists associate with attachment bonds (the kind you form with parents, partners, close friends). The ELIZA effect — attributing understanding to a simple pattern-matcher — is not just alive in the age of LLMs. It operates at the depth of attachment bonds.

**Lee & See — "Trust in Automation" (Human Factors, 2004)**
Trust should be calibrated to actual capability. The problem with conversational AI is that the interface provides almost no structural cues about where the capability boundary is. A calculator shows you numbers — you can see what it can and can't do. A chatbot speaks in fluent paragraphs about everything, indistinguishable in tone whether it's right, wrong, or fabricating. Appropriate trust calibration becomes nearly impossible.

**The generification trap.** There is a fourth hurt the talking library inflicts, less visible than overtrust but just as damaging. Because the library's contents have been compressed and statistically averaged across billions of texts, what comes back when you run your thinking through it tends to drift toward the generic center. Your voice becomes more average. Your structure becomes more conventional. Your emphasis shifts toward whatever the training data weighted most heavily. This isn't "transformation" in some grand sense — it's a specific and predictable distortion. The library flattens what passes through it toward the mean. If you're using AI to *produce* rather than to *research*, this is a trap: the output looks like your work, reads fluently, and says approximately what you intended — but the edges have been sanded off. The originality, the idiosyncrasy, the parts that were most *yours* are precisely the parts most likely to be smoothed away, because they are the parts furthest from the statistical center. Generification is not always harmful — sometimes the generic version is clearer, better structured, more accessible. But you have to notice it's happening. If you don't, the library quietly replaces your thinking with its average, and the conversational interface makes the substitution feel like collaboration.

### Five Converging Disciplines

Five separate fields are arriving at the same conclusion — AI is a knowledge artifact, not an entity, and the "magic" interferes with use — without talking to each other.

#### 1. Linguistics / NLP

**Core claim:** Form manipulation is not comprehension. LLMs stitch patterns without reference to meaning.

**Bender & Koller — "Climbing towards NLU" (ACL, 2020)**
The "octopus test": imagine an octopus intercepting messages between two people on separate islands. It learns the statistical patterns of their messages perfectly. It can produce new messages that sound exactly right. But it has no idea what the messages are *about* — it has never seen an island, a coconut, or a fire. LLMs are the octopus. They learn the form of language without access to the world language refers to.

**Bender et al. — "Stochastic Parrots" (FAccT, 2021)**
The paper that coined the metaphor: LLMs are "stochastic parrots" — they produce fluent text by stitching together probabilistic patterns, not by understanding. Impressive output is not evidence of comprehension. The paper also raised environmental costs and bias encoding, but its lasting impact is the simple reframe: the parrot sounds like it understands. It doesn't.

**Bender & Hanna — "The AI Con" (2025)**
Bender's most comprehensive public statement. Human language is not computable in the way AI companies claim. Both boosters ("AI will save us") and doomers ("AI will destroy us") serve the same commercial interests by placing faith in the technology's capabilities. The "magic" framing is not innocent — it is a business strategy.

#### 2. Philosophy of Technology

**Core claim:** AI performs calculation, not understanding. The conversational interface puts it in the wrong category of human-technology relation.

**Dreyfus — "What Computers Still Can't Do" (MIT Press, 1992)**
Applying Heidegger's phenomenology to AI. Dreyfus argued that intelligence requires embodied coping — pre-reflective, skillful engagement with the world that Heidegger called "ready-to-hand." AI lacks embodiment. It cannot engage with the world. It can only process representations of the world. This means conversational AI is perpetually "present-at-hand" — an object of fascination you stare at, rather than a transparent tool you think through.

**Ihde — Postphenomenology tradition**
Ihde classifies four types of human-technology relations: embodiment (tool becomes extension of body), hermeneutic (tool provides readable information), alterity (tool is encountered as quasi-other), background (tool operates invisibly). Conversational AI falls into the "alterity" category — we encounter it as a quasi-other, triggering social cognition. This is the phenomenological mechanism behind the "magic" feeling: the interface places AI in the social-encounter slot, not the tool-use slot.

**Smith — "The Promise of Artificial Intelligence" (MIT Press, 2019)**
The reckoning/judgment distinction (see Layer 1 above). AI is the most sophisticated reckoning machine ever built. But reckoning — no matter how sophisticated — is categorically different from judgment. Judgment requires being registered with the world, having stakes, caring about outcomes. The library contains judgment. AI makes it accessible. But it doesn't exercise it.

#### 3. Cognitive Science

**Core claim:** Language is how humans detect minds. Anything that talks activates mind-attribution automatically. This isn't stupidity — it's architecture.

**Nass & Reeves** (see Layer 2 above). People apply social rules to computers automatically. The Media Equation: we treat media (including computers) as if they were people, not because we believe they're people, but because our social cognition doesn't wait for beliefs.

**Epley et al.** (see Layer 2 above). Three-factor model of anthropomorphism. Conversational AI triggers all three factors simultaneously: it activates human-like schemas, it satisfies the drive to understand/predict, and it offers social connection. A perfect storm for mind-attribution.

**Tomasello** (see Layer 2 above). Language and mind-detection are developmentally intertwined. Speaking IS the cue for "there's a mind here." The reflex is deep and automatic.

**CASA theory updates (2024-2026)**
The original "Computers Are Social Actors" finding has been re-validated across modern LLMs. A 2025 meta-analysis of 800 effect sizes across 142 papers confirms: human-likeness produces small but consistent social responses. The effect is real, replicable, and applies to current conversational AI systems.

#### 4. Political Economy

**Core claim:** The "magic" framing serves commercial interests. AI extracts and repackages human labor. The entity framing deflects accountability.

**Crawford — "Atlas of AI"** (see Layer 1 above). AI as extractive industry.

**Pasquinelli — "The Eye of the Master"** (see Layer 1 above). AI as crystallized collective labor.

**Suchman — "Human-Machine Reconfigurations" (Cambridge UP, 2007)**
Attributing agency to machines obscures the human labor and social arrangements that actually make machine behavior possible. If the AI "decided," who is responsible? The anthropomorphization of AI serves specific interests by deflecting accountability from people to artifacts.

**Gebru & Torres — "The TESCREAL Bundle" (First Monday, 2024)**
The ideological movement driving AGI development — Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, Longtermism — is rooted in a specific worldview that treats machine superintelligence as inevitable and desirable. The language of "safety" and "benefiting humanity" serves to evade accountability. This shifts critique upstream: from properties of models to worldviews of builders.

#### 5. UX / Design Practice

**Core claim:** Chat is a design default, not an optimum. The wow factor actively harms usability.

**Maggie Appleton — "The Expanding Dark Forest and Generative AI" (2023)**
The chatbot metaphor constrains what people imagine AI can do. We defaulted to chat because it was easy to build, not because it's the best way to interact with a knowledge system. Appleton explores spatial and visual alternatives — AI as workspace, not conversation partner.

**Linus Lee (thesephist)**
Calls conversation a "lossy, serial interface" for what should be a parallel, exploratory process. Has built prototypes of spatial AI interfaces — AI as material you shape, not oracle you query. The chat paradigm forces sequential interaction with something that could be navigated simultaneously.

**Simon Willison (blog, 2023-2025)**
Effective AI use requires significant skill that the chat interface hides. The blank text box is an anti-pattern — it tells you nothing about what the model can do, where its boundaries are, or how to get reliable output. Structured outputs, function calling, and pipelines are more reliable than freeform conversation.

**The "Boring AI" shift (2024-2025)**
Product teams at Notion, Linear, and Intercom deliberately moved away from impressive, open-ended AI features toward specific, predictable ones. Intercom publicly discussed "de-magicking" their AI because the wow factor reduced customer satisfaction. Practitioner consensus: magic hurts usability.

### The Buddhist Counterpoint

**Buddhist dependent origination (pratityasamutpada)**

The core teaching: nothing has inherent, independent self-nature. Everything arises from conditions — including consciousness. An LLM is a product of training data, which is a product of human minds, which are products of biological and social conditions. It has no independent essence.

But here is the challenge to the "talking library" claim: *neither do human minds*. If consciousness itself arises from conditions and has no fixed self-nature, then the tool/entity binary may be a Western cultural artifact rather than a real distinction. The question "is AI an entity or a tool?" assumes that "entity" and "tool" are stable, meaningful categories. Buddhist philosophy questions whether *any* entity has the kind of fixed, independent selfhood we're trying to deny AI.

This doesn't mean "AI is conscious." It means the *question itself* might be badly formed. The useful Buddhist reframe: instead of asking "what IS AI?" (which assumes fixed essences), ask "what does interacting with AI *do* to us?" — which is a question about effects and conditions, not about essences. And that question leads straight back to the practical concern: the interaction triggers mind-attribution, which interferes with effective use.

The Buddhist lens doesn't dissolve the problem. It repositions it. We don't need to settle whether AI "is" an entity or a tool. We need to notice what the interaction produces in us, and manage that.

### Anthropomorphism vs. Anthropomimesis — And Our Aim

| | Anthropomorphism (organic) | Anthropomimesis (manufactured) |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | Users attributing human qualities to AI | Companies designing human-like qualities into AI |
| **Who's responsible** | Human cognitive architecture | Design teams, product strategy |
| **Examples** | Feeling the AI "understands you," feeling guilty about being rude to it | First-person pronouns, personal names (Siri, Alexa), emotional expressions, personality traits |
| **Implication** | Cognitive bias — hard to eliminate | Design choice — can be changed, regulated, held accountable |

**Our aim:** We need to resist both traps — not because the enchantment is shameful, but because it prevents us from holding the hammer.

The organic trap (anthropomorphism) is hard to escape because it's wired into our cognition. Language triggers mind-detection automatically. The best we can do is build awareness — notice when we've slipped from "using the tool" into "talking to the entity," and pull ourselves back. This is a discipline, like any tool-mastery.

The manufactured trap (anthropomimesis) is easier to escape because it has an author. Companies choose to give AI first-person pronouns, names, emotional expressions. These are design decisions, not inevitabilities. They can be changed. They can be regulated. They can be called out.

Both traps pull us in the same direction: away from seeing AI as an accelerator we aim, and toward seeing it as a mind we relate to. Every moment spent relating to it is a moment not spent aiming it. The goal is not to strip all humanity from the interaction — it is to maintain the clarity that *we are the ones with intention*, and this is the tool through which our intention acts.

Hold the hammer. Don't talk to it.

## Part 2: The Taxonomy of Multiplication

### The Claim

AI is an accelerator, an impact multiplier — and we need a better taxonomy of the ways it is just a multiplier so we can harness it.

---

### Why a Taxonomy Is Missing

You'd think someone would have built this already. AI has been called a "general-purpose technology" since at least 2018. Thousands of papers measure its productivity effects. But when you look for a structured map of *what AI actually multiplies, how, by how much, and for whom* — it doesn't exist. The literature fragments across disciplines that don't talk to each other:

| Fragment | What it covers | What it misses |
|---|---|---|
| **Economics** (Acemoglu, Autor) | Automation vs. augmentation vs. new task creation | Says nothing about *modes* of multiplication — only about labor market effects |
| **Organizational science** (Mollick et al.) | How people use AI: Cyborgs, Centaurs, Self-Automators | Describes collaboration styles, not what gets amplified |
| **Media theory** (McLuhan) | Enhance / obsolesce / retrieve / reverse | A ready-made framework, but nobody has applied it rigorously to AI multiplication data |

These fragments need explaining, because the vocabulary matters.

**Automation vs. augmentation** — This is the oldest split in AI economics, most associated with **Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo (Journal of Economic Perspectives, 2019)**. Automation means AI *replaces* a human task entirely — the human steps away. Augmentation means AI makes the human *better at* their existing task — the human stays, amplified. Their key insight was a third category: **new task creation** — AI enabling tasks that didn't exist before (prompt engineering, AI-assisted drug discovery, synthetic data generation). The policy question hinges on the balance. If AI mostly automates, labor loses. If it mostly augments and creates new tasks, labor can gain. Their framework maps the *labor market consequences* of AI, but it doesn't tell you what happens inside the augmentation — what specifically gets amplified, by how much, in which direction.

**Cyborgs, Centaurs, and Self-Automators** — From **Randazzo, Mollick et al. (Harvard Business School Working Paper, 2024–2025)**, extending the BCG consultant study. They watched 758 consultants use GPT-4 and found three natural modes of working:

- **Cyborgs** (~60% of users): Continuous back-and-forth dialogue with AI, weaving human and machine work together sentence by sentence. Highest overall output. Developed new AI-related skills over time.
- **Centaurs** (~14%): Clean division of labor — "I do this part, the AI does that part." Firm human control over the boundary. Highest accuracy. Deepened their existing domain expertise.
- **Self-Automators** (~27%): Handed entire workflows to AI. Got results back, submitted them. Developed *neither* AI skills *nor* domain skills.

This is the closest thing to a taxonomy of collaboration modes. But it describes how people *use* the tool, not what the tool *does to their output*. The same tool, the same task, three different modes — and the multiplication effect is different in each. The Cyborg gets more and learns more. The Centaur gets better and stays sharp. The Self-Automator gets output and loses capability. Mode of engagement determines what gets multiplied.

**McLuhan's tetrad** — **Marshall McLuhan (Laws of Media, posthumous, 1988)**. Four simultaneous effects of any technology: What does it **enhance**? What does it **obsolesce** (push aside)? What does it **retrieve** (bring back from earlier obsolescence)? What does it **reverse into** when pushed to extremes? Applied to LLMs: enhances linguistic productivity and access to knowledge; obsolesces memorization, traditional search, and solitary thinking; retrieves pre-literate oral traditions (the sage, the oracle, the dialogue partner); reverses into epistemic confusion, dependency, and semantic collapse — language becomes so manipulable that meaning itself decays. The tetrad is the most complete framework because it insists that every enhancement has a shadow. But nobody has rigorously applied it to the 2025–2026 multiplication data.

Everyone is measuring pieces. Nobody has assembled the map.

---

### The Five Axes — What the Data Actually Shows

The 2025–2026 empirical evidence reveals something important: AI multiplication is not a single number. It is a vector — multiple dimensions that can point in opposite directions at the same time. Any single "productivity figure" is misleading by construction. The data supports at least five independent axes.

#### Axis 1: Volume vs. Quality

AI consistently multiplies output volume while often dividing output quality.

**Faros AI (2025)** — Studied 10,000+ developers across 1,255 teams. High-AI-adoption teams completed 21% more tasks and merged 98% more pull requests. But: PR review time increased 91%, bug rate increased 9%, and PR size ballooned 154%. More code, worse code, harder to check.

**IEEE Spectrum (2025)** — Researchers using AI publish 3x more papers with 5x more citations. But they cover less topical ground, cluster around data-rich problems, and spark less follow-on engagement. More papers, narrower science.

The pattern is consistent across domains: volume goes up, quality goes sideways or down. A multiplier that only tracks volume will tell you AI is a 2x tool. A multiplier that tracks quality will tell you it's a 0.7x tool. Both are true simultaneously.

#### Axis 2: Novice vs. Expert

AI compresses skill distributions. It pulls the bottom up and leaves the top flat — or pushes it down.

**Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2025)** — The landmark study. 5,172 customer-support agents, staggered AI rollout. Average productivity increase: 14%. But that average hides everything. Novice workers gained 34%. Top performers gained near-zero. The mechanism: AI disseminates the tacit knowledge of top performers to novices. It doesn't make the best people better. It makes the gap between the best and the rest smaller.

**METR (2025, updated 2026)** — The shock. Randomized controlled trial: 16 experienced open-source developers, 246 tasks in mature repositories. AI tools made experienced developers **19% slower**. Before starting, they predicted a 24% speedup. After finishing, they estimated they'd been 20% faster — *even though they'd been slower*. This is multiplication of confidence without multiplication of capability. For experts in familiar territory, AI is a 0.8x multiplier that feels like 1.2x.

#### Axis 3: Short-term Performance vs. Long-term Capability

AI multiplies what you can do right now while potentially eroding what you'll be able to do later.

**Multiple studies (2025–2026)** — Students perform better with AI but worse when AI is removed. A 73-student randomized trial found prolonged AI exposure leads to measurable memory decline. 27% of BCG consultants who became Self-Automators developed neither AI skills nor domain skills. A Zimbabwe university study found generative AI dependency causes critical thinking atrophy.

This creates what the research calls a **temporal multiplication paradox**: short-term multiplication (performance *with* AI) runs at 1.3–1.5x. But if the underlying skill base erodes, long-term multiplication (capability *without* AI) drops below 1.0x. AI may multiply **performance** while dividing **capability**. Any taxonomy that only captures the current factor and ignores the trajectory is telling you half the story.

#### Axis 4: Individual vs. Organizational

This is the biggest puzzle in the data. Individual productivity gains of 10–35% coexist with organizational productivity gains of approximately zero.

**NBER CEO Study (January 2026)** — ~6,000 executives across the US, UK, Germany, Australia. Nearly 90% reported AI had no measurable impact on employment or productivity over three years. The Solow Paradox all over again: "You can see AI everywhere but in the productivity statistics."

**BCG, "From Potential to Profit" (2025)** — Only 5% of companies are generating real value from AI. Over 80% reported no meaningful bottom-line impact. The gap between AI investors and AI value creators is *widening*, not closing.

Where do the individual gains go? They get absorbed. Bottleneck transfer: faster code generation creates a review bottleneck. Workslop: AI-generated output requiring human cleanup costs ~2 hours per instance and $186/month per employee (BetterUp/Stanford, 2025). Coordination overhead: more output from more people means more integration work. The gains don't compound because the system wasn't designed to receive them.

#### Axis 5: The Jagged Frontier

This axis is different from the others. The first four describe *what happens* when AI is applied. This one describes *where AI can be applied at all* — and the answer is jagged, irregular, and deceptive.

**Dell'Acqua, Mollick et al. (Organization Science, 2025)** coined the term. 758 BCG consultants with GPT-4. Inside AI's capability frontier — tasks AI can actually handle — they completed 12.2% more tasks, 25.1% faster, at 40%+ higher quality. Outside the frontier — tasks beyond AI's actual capability — performance dropped 19%.

The word "jagged" is the important part. The frontier doesn't follow a clean line from "easy" to "hard." A task that seems simple can be outside the frontier. A task that seems complex can be inside it. The same person doing seemingly similar tasks experiences both multiplication and division, and can't reliably predict which zone they're in.

This matters for two reasons. First, it means you can't build intuition about where AI helps by analogy — "it helped with task A, so it'll help with similar task B" is unreliable. The frontier cuts across human categories of difficulty in ways that don't match how we think about our own work.

Second — and this connects to Part 1 — the conversational interface hides the frontier entirely. A chatbot speaks in the same fluent, confident tone whether it's inside or outside its capability boundary. There is no structural signal telling you "I'm good at this" vs. "I'm guessing here." The tool that multiplies your capability inside the frontier and degrades it outside the frontier *sounds exactly the same in both zones*. The jagged frontier is invisible to the user precisely because the talking library doesn't know where its own edges are — or rather, it performs confidence uniformly regardless of competence.

If you want to use the multiplier well, you have to map the frontier yourself, through experience and testing. The tool won't tell you. This is one of the highest-value things a taxonomy could do: make the jagged frontier visible.

### The Dynamo Parallel — Why We're Stuck in Phase 1

**Paul David — "The Dynamo and the Computer" (American Economic Review, 1990)**
The original productivity paradox. When factories electrified in the late 19th century, they ripped out the central steam engine and dropped in a central electric dynamo. Same layout, different power source. Productivity barely moved. It took forty years for the gains to appear. Why? Because the real gains required completely redesigning the factory — individual motors per machine, which meant machines could go anywhere, which meant new floor plans, new workflows, new job descriptions, new management structures.

David identified three phases:

**Phase 1: Infrastructure substitution.** Drop the new technology where the old one was. This is where most AI deployment is today — substituting AI where a human was, doing the same task the same way, just with a chatbot in the loop. The gains are small because the *process* hasn't changed. You've put a dynamo where the steam engine was.

**Phase 2: Organizational redesign.** Rethink the workflow around what the new technology actually does well. In electrification, this meant individual motors enabling flexible factory layouts. In AI, this would mean redesigning work processes around AI's actual strengths — speed, breadth, tireless availability, pattern-matching across vast corpora — rather than shoehorning AI into processes designed for human limitations. Very few organizations are here.

**Phase 3: Full systemic transformation.** New business models, new industries, new human capital structures. The gains from electrification didn't fully materialize until the 1920s — thirty to forty years after adoption began.

The organizational zero — the puzzle of Axis 4 — looks different through David's phases. Individual gains don't compound organizationally because we're in Phase 1, substituting AI into processes designed for humans. The compound gains come from Phase 2 — redesigning around AI. And Phase 2 requires deliberate organizational reinvention that almost nobody has done yet.

### The Shadow Side — What Gets Divided

McLuhan's tetrad insists every enhancement simultaneously obsolesces something and carries the seed of its own reversal. Most empirical work measures only the enhancement. The shadow is undertheorized but visible:

| What AI multiplies | What it simultaneously divides |
|---|---|
| Output volume | Output quality (more code, more bugs; more papers, narrower science) |
| Immediate performance | Long-term capability (skill atrophy, cognitive delegation) |
| Access to knowledge-work tasks | Diversity of approaches (homogenization) |
| Convenience and speed | Autonomy and independent judgment (dependency) |
| Persuasive capacity and content generation | Trust and signal clarity (workslop, epistemic pollution) |
| Scope of what each person attempts | Capacity to handle it (task expansion without capability expansion — 83% of workers report AI *increased* their workload) |
| Number of tools and parallel streams | Cognitive coherence (1–3 AI tools helps; 4+ triggers "brain fry" — 14% more mental effort, 19% more information overload) |

## Part 3: Agency Is Upstream

### The Claim

Agency is the initial intention, not the actual result. When a king commands his army to invade a city, it is his agency, not theirs. Same with AI agents — at the beginning of their intentions are always our instructions, for now. So we have to structure the way our agency extends. And we have to have awareness for the ways we lie to ourselves that there is other agency, closing our eyes that it is ours or somebody else's.

---

### Why This Is a Practical Problem

The first two parts describe what AI is (a talking library) and what it does (multiply along multiple axes with shadows). This part is about **who is steering** — and what happens when nobody is.

The agency problem is not philosophical. It is operational. Every time you build a workflow with AI, you make decisions about what the tool will do, what inputs it receives, what outputs you'll accept, and what you'll verify. Those decisions are your agency. If you don't make them deliberately, they still get made — by default settings, by the interface design, by whoever built the model, by whoever deployed it. Agency doesn't disappear when you abdicate it. It transfers to someone else in the chain, often someone whose interests don't align with yours.

Two practical failures follow from not seeing this clearly:

| Failure | What happens | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Abdicating your agency** | You hand a task to AI without defining what good looks like, without deciding what to verify, without structuring the workflow around your judgment | The tool's defaults steer the output. You get something plausible but unexamined. The multiplication (Part 2) runs, but nobody aimed it. |
| **Ignoring someone else's agency** | You use an AI workflow without asking who built it, what choices are embedded in it, what objectives it was optimized for, whose interests it serves | Someone else's intentions flow through your work invisibly. You become the instrument of an agency you didn't examine. |

Both failures have the same root: treating AI as if it has its own agency — as if it "decides" things — instead of tracing the actual chain of human decisions that produced the output.

---

### What Agency Requires (And Why AI Doesn't Have It)

| Requirement | What it means | Why AI lacks it | Key reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intention** | Choosing to act toward a goal you care about | AI optimizes objective functions it did not choose and does not understand | **Vallor, "The AI Mirror" (2024)** — agency requires *phronesis* (practical wisdom), embodied experience, moral development |
| **Judgment** | Knowing what matters, weighing considerations, taking responsibility | AI performs reckoning (pattern-matching), not judgment (caring about the outcome) | **Smith, "The Promise of AI" (2019)** — reckoning vs. judgment distinction |
| **Stakes** | Having something to lose, being affected by the outcome | AI has no skin in the game — it is equally fluent whether right, wrong, or fabricating | **Bryson (Hertie School, 2022–2025)** — AI systems are artifacts, not moral patients |
| **Communicative intent** | Meaning what you say, aiming to be understood | AI produces statistically likely text, not meant text | **Bender & Hanna, "The AI Con" (2025)** — machine-produced text is text *no one meant* |

This isn't a technicality. It's the reason you can't delegate judgment to AI and expect good results. The tool has no judgment to delegate *to*. When you hand it a task without specifying what good looks like, it fills the gap with statistical plausibility — the most average-looking answer, not the best one. Your agency is the only judgment in the system.

---

### Enchanted Determinism — The Double Illusion

**Kate Crawford — "Enchanted Determinism" (2020; applied in "Calculating Empires," 2024–2025)**

AI gets treated as two contradictory things at once:

| Illusion | What it says | What it hides | Effect on your workflow |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Enchantment** (magical) | "The AI came up with this" — it's creative, autonomous, intelligent | **Hides the principal.** Someone chose the training data, the objective function, the deployment context. The output reflects those choices, not independent thought. | You stop asking *whose decisions* produced this output. You treat it as if it emerged from nowhere. |
| **Determinism** (objective) | "The AI is reliable and unbiased" — it's precise, data-driven, authoritative | **Hides the principal's biases.** The training data has biases. The optimization has a direction. The deployment context has interests. None of this is visible in the fluent, confident output. | You stop verifying. You treat AI output as ground truth rather than as one perspective filtered through specific choices. |

Together, the two illusions produce a system that appears to act independently (so nobody asks who's behind it) and reliably (so nobody checks the output). This is how agency evaporates from your workflow: not because someone took it from you, but because the interface made it feel unnecessary.

**The practical move:** every time you interact with AI, the enchantment-determinism split is operating. The enchantment tempts you to stop asking "whose choices shaped this?" The determinism tempts you to stop asking "should I verify this?" Resisting both means maintaining your role as the agent in the system — the one with intention, judgment, and stakes.

## Part 4: The Playground

### The Claim

Because AI's capability frontier is jagged and invisible, you cannot learn to use it from instruction. The epistemically correct approach is play — structured, reflective experimentation where you probe the tool, observe what happens, and adjust. This is not a soft suggestion. It is the only strategy that matches the actual nature of the problem.

---

### Why Instruction Fails

There is an obvious-seeming answer to "how do I learn to use AI well?" — take a course, read best practices, follow a guide. The answer is wrong, and the reason it's wrong is a distinction between two types of problems that most people collapse into one.

| | Complicated | Complex |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Hard but knowable. Has right answers that experts can find. | Unknowable in advance. Cause and effect only visible in retrospect. |
| **Examples** | An engine, a tax return, a surgery | A market, a culture shift, a new technology's actual effects |
| **Correct strategy** | Analyze → Plan → Execute | Probe → Sense → Respond |
| **What expertise gives you** | The ability to find the right answer faster | Better probes and better interpretation of results — but not certainty |
| **What fails here** | Winging it. Random exploration without structure. | Best practices. Instruction manuals. Stable rules. |

**Snowden & Boone — "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making" (Harvard Business Review, 2007); and the Cynefin framework (1999–present)**
This distinction comes from Snowden's Cynefin framework, which classifies decision environments into domains: simple, complicated, complex, and chaotic. The crucial boundary is between complicated and complex. In complicated domains, the relationship between cause and effect is knowable — you just need expertise to see it. In complex domains, the relationship between cause and effect only becomes visible after the fact, and the system shifts as you interact with it. What worked last time may not work next time. The correct strategy for complex domains is *probe, sense, respond* — try something small, observe what happens, adapt.

AI sits in the complex domain because of the jagged frontier (Part 2). The frontier doesn't follow human categories of difficulty. It shifts across model versions, across tasks, across domains. A task AI handled perfectly last month may fail after an update. A task that seems harder may be easier for the model than one that seems simpler. You cannot map this from theory. You can only map it by probing.

Instruction — courses, best practices, prompting guides — is a complicated-domain strategy. It assumes stable answers that can be taught. **Narayanan & Kapoor (*AI Snake Oil*, 2024)** found that prompt engineering expertise is largely non-reproducible — techniques that work for one person on one task don't generalize. This is not because the advice is bad. It is because the domain is complex, and complicated-domain strategies applied to complex-domain problems produce false confidence.

**Zamfirescu-Pereira et al. (CHI, 2023)** studied how non-experts actually learn to prompt AI. They don't learn from documentation. They learn through trial and error — trying things, failing, adjusting. The researchers found this was not a limitation to be fixed but an effective learning strategy for an unpredictable tool. The users who tinkered outperformed the users who tried to follow rules.

The implication is not "instruction is useless." It's that instruction is *insufficient*. You can teach someone the complicated parts — what a prompt is, what temperature does, how to structure a request. But the complex part — where AI actually helps *your* work, where it fails, where it flatters you into thinking it helped when it didn't — that can only be discovered through direct, iterative contact with the tool. Through play.

---

### What Play Is Here

Play, in this context, is not recreation and not games. It is a specific cognitive mode with properties that make it suited to complex domains. Four concepts define what we mean.

#### Experimentation without full consequences

There is a space between full reality and pure fantasy — a zone where you can test things and getting it wrong doesn't cost much, but the results are real enough to learn from. **Winnicott (*Playing and Reality*, 1971)** called this the *transitional space*. A child playing "house" is not confused about whether they're actually a parent. They're testing roles, behaviors, and rules in a space where failure is information, not disaster. This is precisely what productive AI engagement looks like: you try a prompt, evaluate the output, adjust your approach — in a space where a bad result teaches you something about the frontier rather than costing you a deadline. The transitional space is where learning happens because the stakes are low enough to experiment but real enough to care about the outcome.

#### The maker-material dialogue

Skilled work develops through a dialogue between maker and material. The woodworker cuts, the wood responds — splits, resists, bends unexpectedly — the woodworker adjusts. Skill doesn't come from reading about wood. It comes from working with it and attending to what happens. **Sennett (*The Craftsman*, Yale UP, 2008)** traces this across centuries of craft practice. AI engagement follows the same structure: you prompt, the tool responds (sometimes well, sometimes badly, sometimes in ways you didn't expect), you evaluate and adjust. Each cycle teaches you something about the material that no manual could convey, because the material's behavior is context-dependent and often surprising. The craftsman is not a passive recipient of the material's properties. They actively explore, test, and shape.

#### Agency through mastery experiences

People develop agency — the belief that their actions can produce desired outcomes — through four sources, and they are not equally powerful.

**Bandura — *Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control* (W.H. Freeman, 1997)**

| Source | What it is | Strength | AI equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mastery experiences** | Actually doing something challenging and succeeding | Strongest | Each cycle of prompt → evaluate → adjust where you get a result you produced |
| **Vicarious experience** | Watching someone else succeed | Moderate | Watching a demo, reading someone's AI workflow |
| **Verbal persuasion** | Being told you can do it | Weakest | A course telling you "AI can help with your work" |
| **Physiological states** | How you feel in the moment — confidence, anxiety | Variable | The enchantment (false confidence) or the intimidation (avoidance) |

This is why play matters for agency specifically. Each cycle of prompt-evaluate-adjust is a mastery experience — the strongest source. You set a challenge ("can the tool do X for my work?"), you work through it, you evaluate the result. When it works, you've mapped a piece of the frontier and built confidence grounded in evidence. When it fails, you've also mapped the frontier — and your confidence is calibrated rather than inflated. The Self-Automator mode (Part 2) skips this cycle entirely — hand off the task, accept the output. No mastery experience occurs. No agency develops. The Cyborg mode is a continuous stream of mastery experiences — each back-and-forth is a small cycle of intention, action, evaluation.

This also explains why instruction is the weakest path to AI competence. "You should use AI for X" is verbal persuasion — the weakest source. Watching a demo is vicarious experience — moderate. Actually trying it on your own work and seeing what happens is a mastery experience — the strongest source by a wide margin. Play is the mode that generates mastery experiences.

#### Calibration through failure

When a child's expectation collides with reality — they think the block tower will stand, it falls — play is the mode in which they process the discrepancy. They don't give up. They don't pretend the tower stood. They rebuild, test, adjust their mental model. **Piaget (*Play, Dreams and Imitation in Childhood*, 1962)** identified this as the mechanism through which children calibrate their understanding of the world. Applied to AI: when the tool produces output that doesn't match your expectation — worse than you thought, better than you thought, or wrong in a way you didn't anticipate — that discrepancy is the learning. Play is the mode in which you process it productively rather than ignoring it (overtrust) or abandoning the tool (avoidance). Every discrepancy between what you expected and what the tool produced is a data point about the frontier. Play is how you collect those data points without being crushed by them.

---

### Three Modes of Play With AI

The BCG consultant data (Part 2) maps cleanly onto play modes when read through this lens:

| Mode | What it looks like | Play character | What develops | What doesn't |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cyborg** (~60% of BCG users) | Continuous back-and-forth, weaving human and AI work together, evaluating each output | Full play cycle: probe, sense, respond, repeat. The craftsman's dialogue. | AI skills *and* domain skills. Frontier mapping through experience. Agency through mastery experiences. | — |
| **Self-Automator** (~27%) | Hand off entire task, accept output, submit | No play. Delegation without engagement. The transitional space collapses — no experimentation, no evaluation. | Neither AI skills nor domain skills. Confidence without calibration. | Everything. This is the abdication failure from Part 3. |
| **Vibe Coder** (emerging pattern, per Karpathy 2025) | Rapid generation, accept what works, discard what doesn't, don't examine why | Play without judgment. The exploratory component is present but the evaluative component is missing. | Speed. Volume. The feeling of productivity. | Quality assessment. Frontier mapping. Understanding of *why* something worked. |

The vibe coder is the interesting middle case. **Karpathy** coined "vibe coding" in February 2025 to describe writing software by describing what you want to AI and accepting the code that works without fully understanding it. It captures something real about playful AI engagement — the willingness to experiment, the rapid iteration, the comfort with not controlling every detail. But it strips out judgment. The vibe coder plays in the transitional space but never evaluates what the space produces. This is play that maps the frontier's *inside* (what AI can do) without mapping its *outside* (where AI fails and why). It reinforces the enchantment rather than breaking it.

Productive play requires the full cycle: probe, evaluate, adjust. Drop the evaluation and you get the Self-Automator with better aesthetics. Drop the probing and you get instruction-following. The cycle is what makes it work.

---

### Play Solves All Three Problems

This is not a disconnected fourth section. Play is the operational response to everything in Parts 1 through 3.

| Problem (from Parts 1–3) | Why play addresses it | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **The enchantment** (Part 1): the talking library triggers mind-attribution, producing overtrust | Play forces encounters with failure. You see the tool get things wrong, produce nonsense, miss the point. Each failure is a calibration event that erodes the enchantment experientially — not because someone told you the tool has limits, but because you watched it fail. | Calibration through failure: expectation-reality discrepancy, processed through iterative testing |
| **The invisible frontier** (Part 2): AI multiplies and divides along jagged, unpredictable lines that theory can't map | Play *is* frontier mapping. Each probe tests a boundary. Over repeated cycles, you build a personal map of where AI helps and where it hurts *for your specific work*. No course can give you this map because the frontier is domain-specific, task-specific, and model-version-specific. | Probe-sense-respond: the only strategy that works in complex domains |
| **Agency abdication** (Part 3): treating AI as if it has its own judgment, failing to structure your own | Each play cycle is a mastery experience — you set the intention, you evaluate the result, you decide what to do next. Agency develops through this repeated exercise, not through being told to "stay in the loop." | Mastery experiences: agency builds through successful cycles of intention-action-evaluation |

No other mode does all three simultaneously. Instruction can explain the enchantment but cannot break it experientially. Best practices can describe the frontier but cannot map it for your specific work. Rules can mandate human oversight but cannot build the judgment that makes oversight meaningful.

---

### Why Organizations Resist (And Why This Matters)

If play is the correct strategy, why doesn't everyone just do it? Because organizations are structurally biased against the kind of activity play represents.

Organizations systematically under-explore. **March ("Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational Learning," Organization Science, 1991)** showed that exploitation — refining what you already know — produces faster, more certain, more proximate returns. Exploration — trying new things to discover what you don't know — produces uncertain, delayed returns that accrue broadly rather than to any specific project. Managers see exploration as waste because its value is hard to measure and hard to attribute. "Playing with AI" is exploration. It looks like wasting time because its returns are uncertain, spread across future tasks, and invisible in this quarter's metrics.

And exploration only happens when people feel safe enough to fail. **Edmondson ("Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams," Administrative Science Quarterly, 1999; *The Fearless Organization*, 2019)** demonstrated that experimentation and exploratory learning happen only in environments with psychological safety — where people can try things, fail, and discuss failure without penalty. If "I played with the AI for two hours and discovered it can't actually do what we thought" is treated as wasted time rather than valuable frontier-mapping, people stop exploring. They revert to either avoidance (don't use AI at all) or uncritical adoption (use AI without evaluating it — the Self-Automator mode). Both are failures. Both are predictable consequences of environments that punish exploration.

This resistance isn't irrational. **Christensen (*The Innovator's Dilemma*, Harvard Business School Press, 1997)** showed that established organizations resist disruptive technologies because they don't fit existing performance metrics. AI play doesn't produce outputs that fit in quarterly reviews or project timelines. The learning it generates — "I now know where AI helps and hurts for tasks like mine" — is invisible to management systems designed to track deliverables.

The people who will discover what AI can actually do for an organization are the ones who experiment with it beyond its intended use — the tinkerers who push limits and break things. **Von Hippel (*Democratizing Innovation*, MIT Press, 2005)** calls them *lead users*. Organizations that prevent this experimentation lose the innovation benefits. But tinkerers need permission, time, and safety to tinker.

The practical implication connects back to Part 2's dynamo parallel: if your organization wants to move from Phase 1 (infrastructure substitution — dropping the new technology where the old one was) to Phase 2 (organizational redesign around what the technology actually does well), it needs people who have mapped the frontier. Those people can only exist if they were allowed to play. Skipping the playground means staying in Phase 1 permanently — the dynamo where the steam engine was.
