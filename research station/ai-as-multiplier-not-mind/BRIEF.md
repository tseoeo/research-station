# Research Brief: AI as Multiplier, Not Mind — A Framework for What AI Actually Is and How Agency Really Works

## Question

We are proposing three interconnected claims about AI and want to find what existing thinking supports, challenges, expands, or is adjacent to each — with emphasis on both foundational work AND the latest discourse (2024-2026). The three claims are:

1. **AI is a talking library** — it is an amalgam of human knowledge made conversational, not an entity. The "magic" feeling it produces is a UX phenomenon that actively interferes with using it as a tool.
2. **AI is a multiplier, and we lack a taxonomy of multiplication** — if AI is an accelerator/impact multiplier, we need a structured map of *what* it multiplies, *how*, *by how much*, and *for whom*. Without this, we can't aim it.
3. **Agency is upstream, never inside** — when we talk about "AI agents," the agency always traces back to a human principal's intention. The dangerous move is pretending otherwise, because it lets the actual decision-maker hide behind the tool. Like a king commanding an army — it is his agency, not theirs.

We also claim these three are structurally bound: if you mistake the library for a mind (1), you misunderstand what it amplifies (2), and you misattribute who is acting (3).

## What Kind of Answer Is Needed

For EACH of the three topics, we need a structured landscape organized into these categories:

- **Supporting**: Work that directly argues or provides evidence for the claim
- **Challenging**: Work that argues against or complicates the claim (counterarguments, rebuttals)
- **Adjacent**: Work that addresses related but distinct questions, useful as context
- **Expanding**: Work that takes the idea further than we have, or opens new dimensions we haven't considered

For the **fourth layer** (how the three bind together): same structural categories — what existing work treats these as connected? What frameworks unify tool-nature, multiplication dynamics, and agency attribution?

Finally: a **"What Are We Missing"** section — blind spots, angles none of the existing literature covers, questions that should be asked but aren't.

## Scope and Constraints

- Time period: foundational/classic works ARE relevant, but there MUST be strong coverage of 2024-2026 discourse — blog posts, essays, talks, papers, Twitter/X threads from notable thinkers. The conversation is happening NOW and we need to be current.
- Domains to cover: philosophy of technology, philosophy of mind, AI ethics, economics of technology, HCI, legal theory (especially around AI liability/agency), political philosophy (delegation of power), cognitive science, STS (Science and Technology Studies)
- Do NOT limit to academic papers — include public intellectuals, practitioners, essayists, podcasts, notable blog posts
- Both Western and non-Western perspectives where available

## What We Already Know

Starting points we've already identified (workers should go beyond these, not just summarize them):

**Topic 1 (Talking Library):**
- Heidegger's ready-to-hand / present-at-hand distinction (tool transparency vs. dazzlement)
- Bender & Koller's "stochastic parrots" (narrower linguistic version of the claim)
- Andy Clark's extended mind thesis (potential counterargument — the library becomes part of cognition)
- Shannon Vallor on AI and human capacities
- HCI literature on "automation surprise"

**Topic 2 (Taxonomy of Multiplication):**
- Bresnahan & Trajtenberg on general-purpose technologies
- Erik Brynjolfsson's AI productivity research
- Carlota Perez on technological revolutions
- Ethan Mollick's practical AI co-intelligence experiments
- Gap identified: nobody has produced a clean taxonomy of *modes* of multiplication (speed vs. scope vs. quality vs. access)

**Topic 3 (Agency Upstream):**
- Principal-agent theory in economics
- Bruno Latour's actor-network theory (counterargument — tools have agency)
- Langdon Winner, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?"
- Madeleine Clare Elish, "moral crumple zones"
- Meaningful human control debate in autonomous weapons policy

## Suggested Angles

### Angle 1: The Talking Library
Search for: philosophy of AI as tool vs. entity, critiques of AI anthropomorphism, ELIZA effect and its modern equivalents, "stochastic parrots" debate and responses, technology enchantment/disenchantment literature, cultural analysis of why we anthropomorphize software, recent (2024-2026) essays and posts arguing AI is "just" a tool or arguing it is more than one. Look for cognitive science on why conversational interfaces trigger social responses (Nass & Reeves "Media Equation" and its updates). Check for designers and practitioners writing about how the magic feeling hurts usability.

### Angle 2: Taxonomy of Multiplication
Search for: AI as general-purpose technology literature, empirical studies on AI productivity gains by task type (2024-2026), frameworks for categorizing what AI amplifies, "jagged frontier" (Mollick's term) and similar concepts, inequality effects of AI as amplifier (who gets multiplied more?), historical analogies (printing press, electricity, computing) and what taxonomies existed for those. Look for anyone who has attempted to build a structured framework of AI's modes of impact multiplication. Check economic policy papers on differential AI impacts.

### Angle 3: Agency Attribution
Search for: philosophy of AI agency, delegation chains and responsibility, "responsibility gap" in AI ethics, legal frameworks for AI liability and who bears it, political theory on technology as power extension, military/autonomous systems and command responsibility, corporate use of AI to obscure decision-making, recent (2024-2026) discourse on "AI agents" and who is actually the agent. Look for critics of the "AI agent" framing specifically. Check for work connecting AI agency questions to older questions about institutional agency and corporate personhood.

### Angle 4: The Binding Layer
Search for: unified frameworks that connect tool-nature, amplification, and agency attribution. STS (Science and Technology Studies) likely has the most here. Check whether anyone has made the specific argument that anthropomorphism leads to both misunderstanding of amplification effects AND misattribution of agency. Look at technology governance frameworks that address all three. Check recent AI policy/regulation discourse for implicit connections.

### Angle 5: What's Missing
Each worker should note, as they research their angle: what questions are NOT being asked? What perspectives are absent? What would change if we took these three claims seriously as a unified framework — what would we research differently, build differently, regulate differently?

## Specific Questions to Answer

1. Has anyone explicitly argued that the conversational interface of AI is a barrier to effective use (not just a benefit)?
2. Has anyone built a taxonomy of AI's multiplication modes (not just "it makes things faster" but structured categories)?
3. Who is making the strongest argument that AI agency talk is a category error? Who is making the strongest counterargument?
4. Is there existing work that explicitly connects the anthropomorphism problem to the agency attribution problem?
5. What does the latest (2025-2026) discourse look like on each of these — has the conversation shifted as AI tools have become more widespread?
6. Are there non-Western philosophical traditions that frame tool-use, amplification, or delegation differently and usefully?
