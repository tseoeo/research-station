# Angle 3: Agency Is Upstream, Never Inside
# Angle 4: The Binding Layer

## Research Landscape — Structured Findings

**Claim (Angle 3):** When we talk about "AI agents," the agency always traces back to a human principal's intention. The dangerous move is pretending otherwise, because it lets the actual decision-maker hide behind the tool. Like a king commanding an army — it is his agency, not theirs.

**Claim (Angle 4):** Three claims are structurally bound — if you mistake the library for a mind (1), you misunderstand what it amplifies (2), and you misattribute who is acting (3). These are not independent errors but a single interconnected failure mode.

## 3.1 SUPPORTING — Work That Directly Argues Agency Belongs to Humans, Not AI

### Foundational

**Joanna Bryson — "Robots Should Be Slaves" (2010) and subsequent governance work**
Bryson's foundational argument: robots are artifacts, not moral patients, and designing them to appear as peers is ethically irresponsible because it confuses our obligations. She has consistently maintained that AI systems are tools owned by and serving humans, and that attributing agency to them is a category error rooted in anthropomorphism. In 2022–2025 work (Hertie School, Berlin), she extended this to argue that corporate actors *strategically* anthropomorphize AI to diffuse accountability — if "the AI decided," no human is responsible. Her testimony to UK and EU parliamentary bodies reinforces: legal and moral responsibility must remain with human developers, deployers, and owners.
- Venue: Adventures in NI (blog), Hertie School papers, UK/EU parliamentary testimony
- Relevance: Direct support for the "upstream agency" claim; strongest articulation of the tools-not-agents position

**Shannon Vallor — *The AI Mirror* (Oxford University Press, 2024)**
Vallor argues AI systems function as mirrors reflecting human choices, values, and biases — not as independent agents. Treating AI as an agent with its own goals fundamentally mischaracterizes what these systems are and obscures the human decisions embedded in their design, training data, and deployment. Drawing on virtue ethics: genuine agency requires practical wisdom (*phronesis*), embodied experience, and moral development — none of which AI systems possess. Vallor has been particularly vocal that the "AI agent" framing undermines democratic accountability.
- Source: *The AI Mirror* (2024), Edinburgh Futures Institute lectures
- Relevance: Philosophical foundation — agency requires capacities AI categorically lacks

**Madeleine Clare Elish — "Moral Crumple Zones" (2019, widely cited through 2025)**
Introduced "moral crumple zones": situations where a human operator absorbs blame for systemic failures involving automation. Directly addresses how principal-agent relationships get distorted — the nominal human "agent" (e.g., safety driver in an autonomous vehicle) becomes a scapegoat while the actual principal (the corporation) escapes accountability. The crumple zone is the inverse of the upstream-agency claim: agency flows up but *blame* flows down.
- Source: *Engaging Science, Technology, and Society*
- Relevance: Empirical/conceptual demonstration of how agency misattribution creates real harm

**Andreas Matthias — "The Responsibility Gap" (2004)**
Foundational paper arguing machine learning systems that modify their own behavior create a gap in traditional responsibility attribution: the designer cannot fully predict behavior, the user cannot fully control it, the machine cannot be morally responsible. Matthias's "gap" is the *danger* the upstream-agency claim identifies — though Bryson and others challenge its necessity, arguing the gap is artificial and designers/deployers always bear responsibility for choosing to deploy unpredictable systems.
- Source: *Ethics and Information Technology* 6(3), 175–183
- Relevance: The canonical articulation of what goes wrong when agency appears to detach from humans

### Contemporary / 2024–2026

**Arvind Narayanan & Sayash Kapoor — "AI as Normal Technology" (2025)**
Explicitly reject framing AI as "a separate species, a highly autonomous, potentially superintelligent entity." Advocate viewing AI as normal technology comparable to electricity or the internet — "a tool that we can and should remain in control of." Identify the "capability-reliability gap": what AI can technically do versus what it can reliably accomplish. Argue this gap has been the major barrier to building useful AI agents, and that the agent framing inflates capabilities while obscuring limitations.
- Source: Knight First Amendment Institute, Columbia University, April 2025
- URL: https://knightcolumbia.org/content/ai-as-normal-technology
- Relevance: The most direct 2025 articulation of "agency stays with humans, AI is a tool"

**Narayanan & Kapoor — "AI Agents That Matter" (2024)**
Empirical demolition of AI agent hype. Show that current benchmarks focus narrowly on accuracy while ignoring cost; simple tricks outperform complex agent architectures at lower cost; agents overfit to benchmarks and fail in practice. The paper demonstrates that many "agents" that appear autonomous on benchmarks are not useful in reality.
- Source: arXiv:2407.01502, Princeton University
- Relevance: Empirical evidence that "AI agent" claims systematically overstate autonomy and capability

**Narayanan & Kapoor — "Towards a Science of AI Agent Reliability" (2026)**
Extends normal-technology framework to argue the AI industry lacks good tools for measuring agent reliability, or even a definition of reliability, for AI agents. Directly undermines autonomous-agency claims by showing that basic measurement infrastructure doesn't yet exist.
- Source: Princeton CITP, 2026

**Emily Bender — "We Need to Talk About How We Talk About 'AI'" (with Nanna Inie, 2026)**
Identifies three categories of problematic AI language: (1) cognition/emotion terms implying thinking; (2) agent language (systems "helping" or "creating") obscuring human accountability; (3) communication framing ("asking" a system) implying reciprocal intent. Argues these systems produce "text that actually no one is accountable for" and advocates describing "what we use systems to do" rather than attributing capabilities to them.
- Source: TechPolicy.Press, January 2026
- URL: https://www.techpolicy.press/we-need-to-talk-about-how-we-talk-about-ai/

**Bender & Hanna — *The AI Con* (2025)**
Book-length argument that "artificial intelligence" is a misleading marketing umbrella encouraging audiences to infer agency where none exists. Hype "helps the rich get richer by justifying data theft, motivating surveillance capitalism, and devaluing human creativity." Rests on an intentionalist theory of language: machine-produced text is inherently meaningless because machines lack communicative intent.
- Source: Harper/Penguin, 2025
- URL: https://thecon.ai/

**Timnit Gebru — "AI Hype Distracted Us From Real Problems" (2025)**
Argues that describing AI systems as autonomous agents is a deliberate rhetorical strategy — companies discuss systems "as if they are their own thing," distracting from labor exploitation and copyright violations. By framing AI as potentially having "its own mind," the industry redirects focus from corporate accountability toward hypothetical existential risks.
- Source: *Tech Won't Save Us* podcast, Episode 203, 2025

**Gebru & Torres — "The TESCREAL Bundle" (2024)**
Argues the ideological bundle driving AGI development (Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, Longtermism) has eugenicist roots. Attributing superhuman agency to AI systems serves these ideological projects while distracting from present harms to marginalized communities.
- Source: *First Monday*, 2024

**Meredith Whittaker — AI Now Institute / Signal Foundation (2023–2025)**
Argues the "AI agency" framing serves corporate interests as a "distraction machine." When companies say "the AI did it" — whether discrimination, misinformation, or harmful content — they externalize responsibility onto an artifact while retaining profit. "AI is not an agent — it is a product, built by companies, for profit, reflecting their choices."
- Source: AI Now annual reports, keynotes 2023–2025

**Kate Crawford — *Atlas of AI* (2021) and subsequent work**
AI is fundamentally a system of extraction and power, not an autonomous agent. Crawford demonstrates AI systems are material, political, and economic artifacts reflecting creators' interests. The "agentic AI" framing is the latest iteration of a persistent pattern: companies anthropomorphize products to obscure labor, resources, and power dynamics. Crawford with Campolo coined "enchanted determinism" — AI is simultaneously treated as magical/superhuman and deterministic/objective, both attributions obscuring human choices.
- Source: Yale University Press (2021), *Nature* (2024), "Calculating Empires" exhibition (2024–2025)

**Daniel Dennett — "The Problem with Counterfeit People" (2023, final work before death in 2024)**
Warned that AI systems designed to pass as agents constitute fraud against humans interacting with them. Not because AI has genuine agency, but because the *appearance* of agency enables deception and accountability evasion. One of the most dangerous near-term AI risks.
- Source: *The Atlantic*, 2023

**Ben Shneiderman — *Human-Centered AI* (2022) and commentary through 2025**
Persistent argument that framing should always be "human-centered AI" not "AI agents." Every use of agent language obscures the human in the loop and weakens accountability. The 2024–2025 "agentic AI" trend represents a deliberate retreat from human-centered design principles.
- Source: Oxford University Press (2022), subsequent commentary

### Legal / Regulatory Support

**EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)**
Implicitly adopts principal-agent framework: assigns obligations to "providers" (developers) and "deployers" (users), not to AI systems themselves. AI is the instrument through which human obligations are discharged or violated. However, post-enactment analysis reveals the Act was designed for a static compliance model and struggles with dynamic agentic systems (see Challenging).
- Source: Official Journal of the EU, 2024

**Sital Kalantry — "Legal Personhood of Potential People: AI and Embryos" (2025)**
Argues against AI legal personhood on accountability grounds. Granting personhood creates a liability shield: injured parties could only sue the AI entity (which holds no assets) rather than the company that created it. "Piercing the corporate veil" is rarely successful, so personhood leaves victims without remedies while giving companies more risk-taking leeway.
- Source: *California Law Review Online*, November 2025

**Heine & Quintavalla — "Bridging the Accountability Gap" (2024)**
Proposes that expanding established law to cover AI leads to "doctrinal overstretch." Draws on Roman law concepts (law of slaves and animals as quasi-agents) as source for AI accountability *without* full legal personhood. Agency remains upstream with the owner.
- Source: *Legal Studies* (Cambridge), Vol. 44, Issue 1, 2024

**Ryan Abbott — *The Reasonable Robot* (2020, updated)**
Argues for AI legal neutrality — treating AI outputs the same as human outputs for liability — while maintaining responsibility flows to human deployers.

**Jack Balkin — "The Path of Robotics Law" (2015 and subsequent)**
AI should be understood through an "information fiduciary" framework — humans and companies deploying AI owe duties to those affected; AI is the mechanism through which duties are discharged or violated.

**Idaho and Utah legislation (2025–2026)**
Have enacted bills explicitly declaring AI is not a legal person. Legislative codification of upstream agency.

**Daron Acemoglu & Simon Johnson — *Power and Progress* (2023)**
AI understood within existing power structures: humans (specifically corporate executives) are the principals, AI systems are tools extending those principals' intentions. The "autonomous AI agent" framing serves tech companies by obscuring who actually benefits from deployment. Acemoglu (2024 Nobel laureate) brings economics authority to the agency-attribution argument.

### Principal-Agent Theory Directly Applied

**Paulius Cerka, Jurgita Grigiene, Gintare Sirbikyte — AI Liability Framework (2015)**
Proposed framework where AI liability follows the principal-agent model, with the human deployer as principal. Widely cited in EU AI Act discussions.
- Source: *Computer Law & Security Review*

**Gabriel Lima et al. (2023–2025)**
Empirical studies on how people attribute agency, responsibility, and blame to AI. Finding: anthropomorphic framing significantly increases willingness to attribute independent agency to AI while correspondingly *decreasing* responsibility attribution to human decision-makers. Direct empirical evidence for the "hiding behind the tool" mechanism.

## 3.2 CHALLENGING — Work That Argues AI Systems Have or Could Have Genuine Agency

**Yann LeCun — "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence" (2022, updated through 2025)**
LeCun argues current LLMs are NOT genuinely agentic ("glorified autocomplete") but future architectures based on world models, planning, and objective-driven optimization *could* achieve genuine agency. His Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) framework proposes genuine machine agency would require: world model, persistent goals, hierarchical planning, ability to act in the world. *Critical note*: LeCun's position actually *supports* the category-error camp regarding current systems while holding open future possibility.
- Source: Meta AI position paper, NeurIPS/AAAI/WEF talks 2024–2025
- Relevance: Strongest technically-grounded counterargument, but concedes the current point

**Murray Shanahan — "Talking About Large Language Models" (2024)**
Nuanced position: we should be extremely careful about language regarding LLMs, but whether future AI could be genuine agents is genuinely open. Proposes "role-playing" as more accurate metaphor for what LLMs do, while leaving open the philosophical question of agency in sufficiently complex computational systems.
- Source: Imperial College London / DeepMind

**Bruno Latour — Actor-Network Theory (foundational counterargument)**
Latour's ANT framework argues tools/technologies *do* have a form of agency — they reshape what is possible, create new action possibilities, and are not mere passive instruments. A speed bump "acts" to slow traffic. This challenges the clean human-upstream/tool-downstream distinction by distributing agency across networks of human and non-human actors.
- Relevance: The strongest philosophical framework for resisting "agency is only upstream"

**Langdon Winner — "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" (1980)**
Argues technologies embody political choices and exercise political effects independent of their users' intentions. Robert Moses's low bridges exclude buses (and thus poor/minority communities) regardless of who uses the parkway. This suggests agency can be *embedded* in design, complicating the purely-upstream model.
- Relevance: Classic counterargument — agency can inhere in the artifact's structure

**Emergence Arguments (scaling community, 2023–2025)**
Claims from Anthropic, DeepMind, OpenAI about "emergent capabilities" in large models. Argument: sufficiently large models develop capabilities not present in smaller ones, potentially including proto-agentic behaviors. However, this has faced significant pushback — Rylan Schaefer et al. ("Are Emergent Abilities a Mirage?" NeurIPS 2023) argued emergence claims are largely measurement artifacts.
- Relevance: The empirical counterargument, now substantially weakened

**Enactivist Minority Position (Di Paolo, Thompson tradition)**
Some enactivist thinkers argue agency emerges from dynamic coupling between agent and environment. Minority position: sufficiently complex AI systems coupled with environments *could* develop minimal agency. *However*, most enactivists (including Evan Thompson) actually argue *against* AI agency, holding that genuine agency requires autopoiesis (biological self-production), which AI systems lack.
- Relevance: Even the potentially sympathetic philosophical tradition mostly rejects AI agency

**Susan Schneider — *Artificial You* (2019) and subsequent work**
Argues we should remain open to the possibility that sufficiently advanced AI could develop consciousness and agency, though emphasizes this is future possibility, not current reality.

**Hon. Katherine B. Forrest — AI Legal Personhood (2024)**
Does not straightforwardly argue against personhood; warns that early cases will involve tort liability and IP, with deeper ethical dilemmas following. Takes seriously the possibility that AI may develop "some form of sentience" requiring different moral considerations than corporate entities.
- Source: *Yale Law Journal Forum*, Vol. 133, April 2024

**The OECD Agentic AI Taxonomy (2026)**
While not arguing *for* genuine AI agency, the OECD's first major policy document on agentic AI treats the tool-agent distinction as a *spectrum* rather than a binary. Recommends typologies distinguishing systems by level of autonomy, adaptiveness, tool access, and capacity to influence environments. This softens the upstream-only claim.
- Source: OECD AI Papers No. 56, February 2026

## 3.3 ADJACENT — Related Work Providing Useful Context

**Henry Farrell & Cosma Shalizi — AI and Democracy (2024–2025)**
AI systems are better understood as "dumb" components in larger sociotechnical systems rather than as agents. The "AI agent" frame misidentifies where decisions actually happen (institutional structures and human choices) and correspondingly misidentifies what needs to be governed.
- Source: Johns Hopkins/SNF Agora Institute, Carnegie Mellon

**Frank Pasquale — *New Laws of Robotics* (2020) and subsequent work**
Treating AI as agent rather than tool undermines democratic governance. His principles include: AI should supplement rather than replace human professionals, precisely because replacement eliminates accountability. 2024–2025 work focuses on how "agentic AI" narratives in healthcare and finance create accountability gaps in regulated industries.

**Safiya Umoja Noble — *Algorithms of Oppression* (2018) and UCLA work through 2025**
Documents how attributing agency to search algorithms and recommendation systems obscures racist and sexist choices embedded in their design. "The algorithm decided" hides human decisions that produced discriminatory outcomes.

**Evan Selinger & Brett Frischmann — "Re-Engineering Humanity"**
Attributing agency to AI is not just a philosophical error but actively degrades human agency by habituating people to defer to automated systems.

**Stuart Russell — *Human Compatible* (2019, updated 2023)**
Russell's "assistance games" framework rejects the standard model of AI optimizing fixed objectives. Three principles: (1) machine's only objective is to maximize realization of human preferences; (2) machine is initially uncertain about those preferences; (3) ultimate source of information is human behavior. Makes AI fundamentally *deferential* to human judgment. Russell continues warning (TIME100 AI 2025) about companies racing toward AGI without adequate governance.
- Source: Viking/Penguin; TIME100 AI 2025

**Russell — "Ethically Compliant Autonomous Systems under Partial Observability" (ICRA 2024)**
Extends the beneficial-AI framework to autonomous systems operating under uncertainty. Develops formal methods for ensuring AI agents remain ethically compliant even when they cannot fully observe their environment. Agency remains bounded by ethical constraints set by humans.

**The 2024–2025 Corporate "Agent" Pivot**
Every major AI company pivoted to "agent" framing: OpenAI (GPT agents, operator), Anthropic (computer use), Google DeepMind (Gemini agents), Microsoft (Copilot agents). This corporate push was met with significant academic/policy criticism. The core critique: "AI agent" language shifts perceived responsibility from deploying companies to AI systems. If a Copilot agent gives bad legal advice, the framing implies the *agent* made a mistake, not that Microsoft deployed an unreliable product.

**OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications (December 2025)**
First formal taxonomy of risks specific to autonomous AI agents: goal hijacking, tool misuse, identity abuse, memory poisoning, cascading failures, rogue agents. Implicitly distinguishes passive tools from autonomous agents, providing a practical security framework but also reifying the agent metaphor.

**NIST AI Agent Standards Initiative (February 2026)**
First U.S. government program dedicated to interoperability and security standards for agentic AI systems. Focuses on how organizations authenticate and govern agents that "act autonomously on behalf of users." Addresses the principal-agent problem practically: how do you ensure the agent remains tethered to the principal's intentions?

**EU AI Act Agentic Governance Gap**
Post-enactment analysis reveals the Act was designed for static compliance, but agentic AI creates fundamental problems: agents invoke third-party tools not known before runtime, operating under different jurisdictions. Creates "accountability vacuum that neither providers nor deployers can navigate."
- Source: "Agentic Tool Sovereignty" analysis, European Law Blog, 2025–2026

**World Economic Forum — "From Chatbots to Assistants" (March 2026)**
Frames the shift from chatbots (tools) to AI assistants (autonomous actors) as the central policy challenge for 2026. "Agentic AI (systems that act, not just answer) will stress-test 'human oversight' rules."

## 3.4 EXPANDING — Work That Takes the Idea Further

**Kate Crawford & Vladan Joler — "Calculating Empires" (2024–2025)**
Massive visualization spanning 1500–2025 mapping how computational power and imperial power are intertwined. Situates "autonomous AI" as the latest iteration of technologies concentrating wealth and control while displacing accountability. This *expands* the upstream-agency claim: the agency isn't just upstream to an individual human — it's upstream to *systems of power* that predate computing.
- Source: Venice Architecture Biennale (Silver Lion 2025), Boghossian Art Prize 2024

**Abeba Birhane — Relational Ethics (2021–2025)**
Argues from Afro-feminist and Merleau-Pontian tradition that agency is fundamentally relational and embodied. Western AI discourse commits a category error by abstracting "agency" from relational, social, and embodied context, then projecting it onto statistical pattern-matching. This expands the claim: the problem isn't just that we put agency in the wrong place (AI instead of humans) — it's that we misunderstand agency itself as detachable from relational context.
- Source: Trinity College Dublin, Mozilla Foundation, *Nature Machine Intelligence*

**Emily Bender — "Resisting Dehumanization in the Age of 'AI'" (2024)**
Identifies six forms of dehumanization in AI discourse, including computational metaphors equating brains with computers, ignoring hidden labor, and treating datasets as "representative." The anthropomorphic language about AI *dehumanizes people* by elevating machine outputs while devaluing human cognition and labor. This expands the agency claim: misattributing agency to AI isn't just a philosophical error — it's an act of dehumanization directed at real people.
- Source: *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 2024

**Bender et al. — "From 'AI' to Probabilistic Automation" (FAccT 2024)**
Empirical study demonstrating anthropomorphic descriptions lead to measurably increased trust. Argues for "probabilistic automation" rather than "AI" to reduce hype-driven trust inflation. Expands the argument by providing *empirical* evidence that language choices directly cause agency misattribution.

# ANGLE 4: THE BINDING LAYER

---

**Core thesis:** If you mistake the library for a mind (Claim 1 / anthropomorphism), you necessarily misunderstand what it amplifies (Claim 2 / multiplication), and you necessarily misattribute who is acting (Claim 3 / agency). These are not three independent errors but a single interconnected failure mode.

---

## 4.1 SUPPORTING — Work That Connects These Problems

### Closest to the Full Binding Thesis

**Adriana Placani — "Anthropomorphism in AI: Hype and Fallacy" (2024)**
*This is the single closest paper to the binding thesis.* Placani argues anthropomorphism operates in two registers simultaneously: as *hype*, it exaggerates AI capabilities (connecting Claim 1 → Claim 2); as *fallacy*, it distorts moral judgments — specifically judgments of moral character, status, responsibility, and trust (connecting Claim 1 → Claim 3). Through anthropomorphization, AIs can be attributed qualities that render a verdict of moral agency plausible when it is not. Covers two of three binding links explicitly.
- Source: *AI and Ethics* 4, 691–698
- URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-024-00419-4
- Gap: Does not formalize the three-part structure or identify the Claim 2 → Claim 3 link

**Shannon Vallor — *The AI Mirror* (2024) + "Find the Gap" (with Vierkant, 2024)**
Vallor's corpus addresses all three claims: AI is a "statistical mirror" of human thought, devoid of autonomous agency (Claim 1/anti-anthropomorphism); it "amplifies and conditions" human cognition rather than operating independently (Claim 2); and this narrowing perpetuates existing power asymmetries through misattributed agency (Claim 3). Her work with Vierkant addresses the responsibility gap, arguing epistemic opacity and attenuated behavior control are exacerbated by AI but not unique to it. *Addresses all three but does not frame them as a single structural argument.*
- Source: OUP (2024); PhilPapers (Vallor & Vierkant 2024)

**Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, Mitchell — "Stochastic Parrots" (FAccT 2021)**
The foundational paper implicitly binds all three: LLMs are not minds (anti-anthropomorphism / Claim 1), they produce statistically plausible text without understanding (what's actually being amplified / Claim 2), and therefore attributing agency to them is a category error (Claim 3). *However, the binding is implicit, not explicit.*
- Source: ACM FAccT 2021

### Explicit Two-Way Connections

**Akbulut, Weidinger, Manzini, Gabriel, Rieser — "All Too Human? Mapping and Mitigating Risk from Anthropomorphic AI" (AIES 2024)**
Maps risks from anthropomorphic design features, showing how they create over-reliance, emotional attachment, and infringing on user autonomy. Connects anthropomorphic design choices → users forming parasocial relationships → distorted understanding of what the system can do (Claim 2) and who is responsible for outputs (Claim 3).
- Source: AAAI/ACM AIES 2024, 7(1), 13–26
- URL: https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AIES/article/view/31613

**Takuya Maeda — "Misplaced Capabilities" (AIES 2024)**
The title captures the Claim 1 → Claim 2 link: anthropomorphism leads to "misplaced" capability attributions. Categorizes types of anthropomorphism in LLMs and argues they co-create misplaced trust.
- Source: AAAI/ACM AIES 2024, 7(2), 35–36

**Maeda & Quan-Haase — "When Human-AI Interactions Become Parasocial" (FAccT 2024)**
Develops conceptual framework showing how chatbot "roleplaying" and user role projection co-produce parasocial dynamics. Ethical concerns include illusions of reciprocal engagement, task misalignment, and information leaks — all consequences of anthropomorphism leading to agency misattribution.
- Source: ACM FAccT 2024

**FAccT 2024 — "From 'AI' to Probabilistic Automation"**
Empirically tests how anthropomorphic language affects trust across four categories: properties of a cognizer, agency, biological metaphors, properties of a communicator. Finding: even naming a system "AI" triggers anthropomorphic trust responses. Supports the structural link between Claim 1 and Claims 2–3.
- Source: ACM FAccT 2024

**Gabriel Lima et al. — Empirical Studies (2023–2025)**
Demonstrate that anthropomorphic framing increases willingness to attribute independent agency to AI *and* correspondingly decreases responsibility attribution to human decision-makers. Direct empirical evidence for the Claim 1 → Claim 3 causal link.

### Supporting the Structural Necessity

**Santoni de Sio & Mecacci — "Four Responsibility Gaps" (2021)**
Argue the responsibility gap is "not one problem but a set of at least four interconnected problems" — gaps in culpability, moral accountability, public accountability, and active responsibility. Their insistence that these gaps are structurally interconnected (caused by different but interacting sources: technical, organisational, legal, ethical, societal) parallels the binding argument.
- Source: *Philosophy and Technology* 34(4), 1057–1084

**Mark Coeckelbergh — "AI, Responsibility Attribution, and a Relational Justification of Explainability" (2020)**
AI technologies do not meet criteria for full moral agency (freedom and consciousness) and therefore cannot be held responsible. Proposes relational framework where responsibility distributes across human actors. Touches all three claims but frames through explainability rather than structural binding.
- Source: *Science and Engineering Ethics*

## 4.2 CHALLENGING — Work That Resists the Binding

**Latour's Actor-Network Theory (and STS tradition)**
If agency is genuinely distributed across networks of human and non-human actors (as ANT holds), then the three claims may not bind as described. A Latourian would argue: the tool *does* partially constitute the mind that uses it, the multiplication is a property of the *network* not any individual actor, and agency is always already distributed. The binding thesis presupposes a clearer human/tool boundary than ANT permits.
- Relevance: The strongest theoretical challenge to the binding structure itself

**Extended Mind Thesis (Andy Clark, David Chalmers)**
If cognitive tools genuinely become part of the mind (as the extended mind thesis holds), then "mistaking the library for a mind" may not be a mistake at all — the library *is* part of the extended mind. This potentially breaks Claim 1, which would unravel the binding.
- Relevance: Challenges Claim 1, which the binding depends on

**LeCun's Future-Agency Argument**
If future AI architectures could achieve genuine agency, then the binding is temporally contingent — true now but not necessarily always. This doesn't refute the binding for current systems but limits its scope to a historical moment rather than a structural necessity.

**Emergence / Scaling Arguments**
If genuinely novel capabilities emerge at scale, the relationship between what humans put in and what comes out may not be straightforwardly "amplification." This would challenge Claim 2 and weaken the binding. *However*: Schaefer et al. (NeurIPS 2023) substantially undermined emergence claims.

## 4.3 ADJACENT — Related Frameworks

### Non-Western Philosophical Traditions

**Buddhist Philosophy — Anattā (No-Self)**

*Nikki Mirghafori — "A Middle Path for AI Ethics?" (2024)*
Argues the doctrine of anattā means there is no fixed, abiding essence in any entity — including AI. "The ontological primacy of relational dynamics calls into question the presumed independence of human and artificial intelligences" and questions "the reduction of technologies (distributed relational systems) to tools (localizable artifacts)." This directly challenges the simplistic tool/agent binary — and offers a *different dissolution* of the binding: not that AI has agency, but that *no entity* has the kind of fixed agency the Western debate assumes.
- Source: *Theology and Science* 23(1), 2024

*Ven. Juewei Shi* (former AI developer, now Buddhist nun at Nan Tien Institute)
Bridges technical AI development and Buddhist ethics. Applies no-self doctrine to argue AI should not be treated as an autonomous moral subject but as "a contingent mirror of human data, design, and intention." Resonates with Vallor's "mirror" metaphor.

*Frontiers in Psychology (2025)* — "The Anthropomorphization of AI and the Concept of Buddhist Compassion"
Buddhist anattā-based compassion offers "an anthropocentrism-transcending perspective for robot ethics," proposing the non-self doctrine as computable technical ethics framework.

*Religions (MDPI, 2026)* — "Between No-Self and the Algorithm"
Buddhist mind-nature as alternative ethical architecture for AI and human self-realization.

**Confucian Ethics — Role, Hierarchy, Delegation**

*"Natural Intelligence, Not Artificial: A Confucian Reframing" (2026)*
Generative AI understood as "an extension of human creativity operating within human-guided ethical judgment and responsibility," drawing on *shengsheng* (creative transformation), *ren* (co-humanity). Directly supports Claim 2 (AI as amplifier) and Claim 3 (responsibility remains with humans).
- Source: Taylor & Francis, 2026

*Tao Jiang et al. — "Ethics, Society, and Technology: A Confucian Role Ethics Perspective" (2021)*
Confucian role ethics, grounded in relational personhood, provides framework where AI cannot occupy moral roles because roles require reciprocal relational capacity. Delegation is always hierarchical and the delegator retains responsibility. Directly supports the upstream-agency claim through a non-Western philosophical tradition.
- Source: *Technology in Society*

*"Confucian Role Ethics and Artificial Intelligence" (2025)*
Confucian approaches have an easier time incorporating AI into analysis of virtue than Western theistic approaches, because role ethics provides distinctive virtues for particular roles — and AI cannot authentically fill a role requiring relational reciprocity.
- Source: Academia.edu

**Ubuntu Philosophy — Relational Agency**

*Sabelo Mhlambi — "From Rationality to Relationality" (2020)*
Foundational text. Ubuntu philosophy reveals AI harms as "violations of ubuntu's relational personhood and relational model of the universe." The shift from rationality to relationality as benchmark for personhood means AI cannot be a person (Claims 1/3) and governance must center on human relational responsibilities (Claims 2/3).
- Source: Carr Center Discussion Paper 2020-009, Harvard Kennedy School

*Ogunniye et al. — Ubuntu in Global AI Inclusion (2022)*
Ubuntu emphasizes communal personhood, relational autonomy, shared responsibility as critique of Eurocentric bias in mainstream AI ethics.
- Source: *Patterns* (Cell Press)

*Springer (2025)* — "Ethics of AI in Africa: Interrogating Ubuntu and AI Governance"
Critiques how Ubuntu is invoked in AI ethics — whether it genuinely informs governance or is being instrumentalized.

**Hindu/Vedantic Philosophy — Kartā and Consciousness**

*Cambridge Companion to Religion and AI — "Hinduism and Artificial Intelligence"*
Vedantic philosophy: consciousness (*Chaitanya*) is "not an emergent property of biological complexity but the foundational reality itself — eternal, uncreated, and boundless," while AI is "a product of conditioned causality." The concept of *kartā* (doer/agent) in Samkhya-Vedanta requires intentionality grounded in consciousness, which AI lacks by definition. Potentially the strongest non-Western support for Claim 3.
- Source: Cambridge University Press

*IJRTSSH (2025)* — Hindu Responses to AI
Vedantic *chetana* (awareness) can exist in degrees, allowing functional moral consideration of AI without requiring proof of subjective qualia. Nuances Claim 1 by suggesting graduated rather than binary distinction.

*Future of Life Institute* — "A Hindu Perspective on AI Risks"
Emphasizes *samatva* (harmony), *santulan* (balance), *sahcharya* (interconnectedness) as ethical coordinates for AI, framing technology as embedded in relational webs.

**Indigenous Knowledge Systems**

*Jason Edward Lewis, Hemi Whaanga, Ceyda Yolgormez — "Abundant Intelligences" (2024)*
Six-year research program argues Western AI development "suffers from fundamental epistemological shortcomings" rooted in rationalist epistemologies "excluding many ways of knowing." Indigenous knowledge systems contain frameworks for understanding technology as integrated into existing lifeways rather than as autonomous agents.
- Source: *AI & Society* 40, 2141–2157

*"Incorporating Indigenous Knowledge Systems into AI Governance" (2024)*
Applies Kaitiakitanga (Māori guardianship) and Hozho (Navajo harmony/balance) to AI governance, emphasizing collective stewardship over individual ownership of technological outcomes.

### Governance Frameworks (Adjacent but Incomplete)

**UNESCO Recommendation on Ethics of AI (2021, monitoring ongoing)**
Most comprehensive governance document for the binding thesis. Explicitly states member states shall "implement policies to raise awareness about the anthropomorphization of AI technologies" (Claim 1). Insists "An AI system can never replace ultimate human responsibility and accountability" (Claim 3). Frames AI within human oversight requirements (Claim 2). Also incorporates Ubuntu philosophy. *However*: treats the three as separate regulatory concerns, not structural binding.

**EU AI Act — Article 50 Transparency Obligations**
Mandates providers ensure users know they're interacting with AI (addresses Claim 1). But does not address amplification misunderstanding (Claim 2) or agency misattribution (Claim 3) as *structural* problems — treats them as transparency/disclosure issues solvable through labeling.

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework (1.0, updated 2025)**
"Map" function includes identifying "who or what could be harmed" (Claim 3). Does not explicitly address anthropomorphism or treat the three as interconnected.

**Key governance gap:** No governance framework treats the three problems as structurally bound. They address anthropomorphism (through transparency), agency (through human oversight), and sometimes capability misunderstanding (through risk assessment), but as *separate regulatory concerns* rather than manifestations of a single underlying error.

## 4.4 EXPANDING — Work That Opens New Dimensions

**Crawford's "Enchanted Determinism" (2020, applied 2024–2025)**
AI is simultaneously treated as magical (autonomous, creative, intelligent) and reliable (deterministic, objective). Both attributions obscure human choices. This expands the binding thesis: the *specific mechanism* by which anthropomorphism (Claim 1) produces amplification misunderstanding (Claim 2) and agency misattribution (Claim 3) is the dual illusion of enchantment + determinism. The "enchantment" grants false autonomy; the "determinism" grants false objectivity.

**Birhane's Relational Challenge to the Agency Concept Itself**
If agency is fundamentally relational and embodied (not a property an individual entity "has"), then the entire Western debate about whether AI "has" agency is malformed. This doesn't just support the upstream-agency claim — it dissolves the framework within which the question is asked. The binding thesis might be strengthened: the reason the three errors bind is that they all stem from the same Western-Enlightenment misconception of agency as an individual possession.

**Buddhist Anattā as Alternative Dissolution**
If no entity has a fixed self, then the question "does AI have agency?" is malformed for a different reason than Birhane's. Buddhist philosophy suggests the binding might be even deeper than claimed: not just three errors flowing from one, but all three as symptoms of a more fundamental misunderstanding about the nature of selfhood and action itself.

**Confucian Role Ethics and the Binding**
Confucian tradition may offer the strongest non-Western *support* for the binding: in a role-ethics framework, you cannot separate what something *is* (its nature/role) from what it *does* (its function/amplification) from who bears responsibility (the role-holder and those above them in the relational hierarchy). The three claims are bound because role, function, and responsibility are never separate in Confucian ethics.

# SPECIFIC QUESTIONS — ANSWERS

---

## Q1: Who is making the strongest argument that AI agency talk is a category error? Strongest counterargument?

**Strongest for category error:** Emily Bender, especially "We Need to Talk About How We Talk About 'AI'" (2026) and *The AI Con* (2025). Bender's argument is the most systematic: she identifies *specific linguistic mechanisms* (cognition terms, agent language, communication framing) that produce the category error, grounds it in philosophy of language (intentionalist theory of meaning), and provides *empirical evidence* (FAccT 2024 probabilistic-automation study) that the language choices causally produce misattribution. Vallor's *The AI Mirror* (2024) is the most philosophically developed version.

**Strongest counterargument:** Bruno Latour's Actor-Network Theory (foundational) remains the strongest *theoretical* counterargument — agency is always distributed across networks, never cleanly upstream. For future-oriented counterargument: LeCun's JEPA framework is the most technically specific, though he concedes current systems lack agency.

## Q2: Is there existing work explicitly connecting the anthropomorphism problem to the agency attribution problem?

**Yes, substantially.** Placani (2024) is the most explicit, arguing anthropomorphism operates as both hype (inflating capabilities) and fallacy (distorting moral judgments including responsibility). Akbulut et al. (AIES 2024) maps the risk pathway from anthropomorphic design → parasocial relationships → distorted agency attribution. Lima et al. (2023–2025) provides empirical evidence that anthropomorphic framing causally increases agency misattribution. Bender (FAccT 2024) shows even the word "AI" triggers anthropomorphic responses that increase trust.

**However:** No one has formalized the *three-part structural binding* (anthropomorphism → amplification misunderstanding → agency misattribution as a single error). This appears to be a genuine gap and potential contribution.

## Q3: What does 2025–2026 discourse look like on AI agents?

**Major shift.** The 2024–2025 corporate pivot to "agentic AI" (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft) triggered a counter-discourse:

- **Narayanan & Kapoor** (2025–2026) established the "AI as normal technology" and "capability-reliability gap" frameworks, empirically demonstrating agent benchmarks overstate real-world performance
- **Bender & Inie** (2026) provided systematic taxonomy of harmful agent language
- **OECD** (2026) published first major policy document attempting to define the tool-agent spectrum
- **NIST** (2026) launched first U.S. standards initiative specifically for agentic AI
- **EU AI Act** analysis (2025–2026) revealed the Act is structurally unprepared for dynamic agentic systems
- **OWASP** (2025) published first security risk taxonomy for agentic applications

The discourse has bifurcated: industry doubles down on "agent" framing while critical scholars, policy bodies, and security researchers increasingly treat it as misleading or dangerous.

## Q4: Non-Western philosophical traditions on delegation, agency, tool-use?

**Substantial resources found across four traditions:**

- **Buddhist anattā** challenges the entire framework by questioning whether any entity has the kind of fixed agency the debate assumes (Mirghafori 2024, Ven. Juewei Shi)
- **Confucian role ethics** provides strong support for upstream agency through relational hierarchy — the delegator always retains responsibility, and roles require reciprocity AI cannot provide (Jiang et al. 2021, 2025 role ethics papers)
- **Ubuntu philosophy** reframes agency as relational and communal, making individual AI "agency" a category error for different reasons than Western arguments (Mhlambi 2020, Ogunniye et al. 2022)
- **Hindu/Vedantic kartā** requires consciousness-grounded intentionality for agency, which AI categorically lacks (Cambridge Companion; IJRTSSH 2025)
- **Indigenous knowledge systems** frame technology as integrated into lifeways, not as autonomous — collective stewardship over individual ownership (Lewis et al. 2024)

# GAPS — WHAT IS NOT BEING ASKED

---

## Gap 1: The Three-Part Structural Binding Itself

No existing work treats anthropomorphism, amplification misunderstanding, and agency misattribution as *the same error at different levels of description.* Placani (2024) connects two; Vallor (2024) addresses all three thematically; but the explicit structural argument — that these are not three problems requiring three solutions but one interconnected failure mode — appears to be a **genuine contribution.**

## Gap 2: Whether Non-Western Traditions Dissolve Rather Than Solve

Current discourse applies non-Western philosophy to the AI ethics debate as formulated in Western terms. The deeper question: do Buddhist anattā, Ubuntu relationality, or Confucian role ethics *dissolve* the three-part problem rather than solving it? If agency was never an individual possession in the first place, the entire debate may be malformed — not just the attribution of agency to AI, but the way we conceptualize human agency upstream.

## Gap 3: Transparency/Disclosure as Reinforcement of the Binary

The governance impulse to address anthropomorphism through disclosure ("tell users it's AI") may actually *reinforce* the binary framing that causes the errors. Disclosing "you are talking to an AI" presupposes a meaningful distinction between talking to a mind and talking to a not-mind — which may entrench rather than resolve the anthropomorphism problem.

## Gap 4: Amplification of Structural Power vs. Individual Signal

Is "AI as amplifier" itself insufficient when the amplification is not of a pre-existing signal but of a *structural power asymmetry*? If AI doesn't just multiply what you give it but preferentially multiplies what already has power (capital, data, institutional backing), then the metaphor of "multiplier" understates the political dynamics.

## Gap 5: The Empirical-Philosophical Disconnect

Empirical work (Lima et al., Bender FAccT 2024) demonstrates that anthropomorphic language *causally* increases agency misattribution. Philosophical work (Vallor, Bryson, Birhane) argues this is a *conceptual* error. These two literatures rarely engage each other directly. A framework bridging the empirical mechanism (language → cognition → misattribution) with the philosophical analysis (category error → accountability failure) would strengthen both.

## Gap 6: What Would Change If We Took the Binding Seriously

If the three claims are genuinely bound, the practical implications are underexplored:

- **For governance:** You cannot solve the agency problem (regulation, liability) without also solving the anthropomorphism problem (interface design, language) and the amplification problem (who benefits, how much, at whose expense). Current governance treats these separately.
- **For design:** Interface choices that trigger anthropomorphism don't just create a UX problem — they create an accountability problem and a distribution-of-benefits problem.
- **For research:** Studying AI "capabilities" without studying who those capabilities amplify and who gets credit/blame for their exercise produces incomplete and potentially misleading findings.

## Gap 7: The Over-Technologization of Ethics

Meta-critique from *AI and Ethics* (2025), "Rethinking Responsible AI," argues current approaches are "over-technologised and under-politicised, leaning on governance-through-design without governance-through-democracy." The three-part binding suggests why: if you treat anthropomorphism as a design problem, amplification as a measurement problem, and agency as a legal problem, you get technical solutions to what is actually a *unified political problem* about who wields power through AI and how they obscure that wielding.

## Gap 8: The Principles-Practice Gap

Jobin, Ienca, & Vayena (2019) and subsequent reviews of 200+ guidelines find convergence on principles (transparency, justice, non-maleficence, responsibility, privacy) but "substantive divergence in how principles are interpreted" and "a gap between principles and actual application." The binding thesis suggests this gap is structural: principles address the three problems separately, but they bind in practice.
