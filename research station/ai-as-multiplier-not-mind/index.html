Part 1

The Talking Library

The Claim
AI as it was created now is just a big talking library — an amalgam of most of our knowledge, put together and made to talk to us. As such, we are taken with the magic of it, which makes it harder to use it as a tool.

The Buddhist Counterpoint

Dependent originationThe Buddhist teaching (pratityasamutpada) that nothing has inherent, independent self-nature. Everything arises from conditions — including consciousness itself. — the core Buddhist teaching — holds that nothing has inherent self-nature. An LLM arises from training data, which arises from human minds, which arise from conditions. It has no independent essence.

But here is the challenge: neither do human minds. If consciousness itself arises from conditions, the tool/entity binary may be a Western cultural artifact. The question “is AI an entity or a tool?” assumes those are stable categories.

The useful Buddhist reframe: instead of asking “what is AI?” (which assumes fixed essences), ask “what does interacting with AI do to us?” — a question about effects and conditions. And that leads back to the practical concern: the interaction triggers mind-attribution, which interferes with effective use.

The Buddhist lens doesn’t dissolve the problem. It repositions it. We don’t need to settle whether AI “is” an entity or a tool. We need to notice what the interaction produces in us, and manage that.

Anthropomorphism vs. Anthropomimesis — And Our Aim

Anthropomorphism (organic)

What: Users attributing human qualities to AI

Responsible: Human cognitive architecture

Examples: Feeling the AI “understands you,” feeling guilty about being rude to it

Implication: Cognitive bias — hard to eliminate

Anthropomimesis (manufactured)

What: Companies designing human-like qualities into AI

Responsible: Design teams, product strategy

Examples: First-person pronouns, names (Siri, Alexa), emotional expressions

Implication: Design choice — can be changed, regulated

Our aim: resist both traps — not because the enchantment is shameful, but because it prevents us from holding the hammer.

The organic trap is hard to escape because it’s wired in. Language triggers mind-detection automatically. The best we can do is build awareness — notice when we’ve slipped from “using the tool” to “talking to the entity” and pull back. This is a discipline, like any tool-mastery.

The manufactured trap is easier to escape because it has an author. Companies choose to give AI first-person pronouns, names, emotional expressions. These are design decisions. They can be changed. They can be regulated.

Hold the hammer. Don’t talk to it.

The Taxonomy of Multiplication

The Claim
AI is an accelerator, an impact multiplier — and we need a better taxonomy of the ways it is just a multiplier so we can harness it.

Why a Taxonomy Is Missing

AI has been called a general-purpose technologyA technology (like electricity or the printing press) that affects an entire economy, can be improved over time, and spawns complementary innovations. AI fits the definition but the label says nothing about how it actually multiplies. since at least 2018. Thousands of papers measure its effects. But a structured map of what AI actually multiplies, how, by how much, and for whom doesn’t exist.

Part 3

Agency Is Upstream

The Claim
Agency is the initial intention, not the actual result. When a king commands his army to invade a city, it is his agency, not theirs. Same with AI agents — at the beginning of their intentions are always our instructions, for now. So we have to structure the way our agency extends. And we have to have awareness for the ways we lie to ourselves that there is other agency.

Why This Is a Practical Problem

The first two parts describe what AI is and what it does. This part is about who is steering — and what happens when nobody is.

Every time you build a workflow with AI, you make decisions: what inputs, what outputs, what to verify. Those decisions are your agency. If you don’t make them deliberately, they still get made — by default settings, by the interface, by whoever built the model. Agency doesn’t disappear when you abdicate it. It transfers.

Failure 1: Abdicating your agency

Hand a task to AI without defining what good looks like, without deciding what to verify. The tool’s defaults steer the output. The multiplication runs, but nobody aimed it.

Failure 2: Ignoring someone else’s agency

Use an AI workflow without asking who built it, what objectives it was optimized for, whose interests it serves. Someone else’s intentions flow through your work invisibly.

Both failures have the same root: treating AI as if it “decides” things — instead of tracing the actual chain of human decisions that produced the output.

Enchanted Determinism — The Double Illusion

Kate Crawford — “Enchanted Determinism” (2020)Crawford identified that AI gets treated as two contradictory things at once: magical (creative, autonomous, intelligent) and deterministic (reliable, unbiased, authoritative). Together, the two illusions produce a system that appears to act independently (so nobody asks who's behind it) and reliably (so nobody checks the output). Applied in "Calculating Empires," 2024–2025. identified that AI gets treated as two contradictory things at once:

The Enchantment Illusion

“The AI came up with this”

Says: It’s creative, autonomous, intelligent

Hides: Someone chose the training data, objective function, deployment context

Effect: You stop asking whose decisions produced this output

The Determinism Illusion

“The AI is reliable”

Says: It’s precise, data-driven, authoritative

Hides: Training data biases, optimization direction, deployment interests

Effect: You stop verifying. You treat output as ground truth.

Together: a system that appears to act independently (so nobody asks who’s behind it) and reliably (so nobody checks the output). This is how agency evaporates — not because someone took it, but because the interface made it feel unnecessary.

The practical move: every time you interact with AI, both illusions are operating. The enchantment tempts you to stop asking “whose choices shaped this?” The determinism tempts you to stop asking “should I verify this?” Resisting both means maintaining your role as the agent — the one with intention, judgment, and stakes.

The Self-Reinforcing Loop

The four parts reinforce each other. Seeing AI as a mind (Part 1) makes you miss that multiplication runs in multiple directions (Part 2), which makes it easy to stop steering (Part 3) — which sends you back to the comfortable illusion that the mind is handling it. Part 4

The Playground

The Claim
Because AI’s capability frontier is jagged and invisible, you cannot learn to use it from instruction. The epistemically correct approach is play — structured, reflective experimentation where you probe the tool, observe what happens, and adjust. This is not a soft suggestion. It is the only strategy that matches the actual nature of the problem.

Why Instruction Fails

There is an obvious-seeming answer to “how do I learn to use AI well?” — take a course, read best practices, follow a guide. The answer is wrong, and the reason it’s wrong is a distinction between two types of problems that most people collapse into one.

Complicated

Hard but knowable

  • Nature: Has right answers that experts can find
  • Examples: An engine, a tax return, a surgery
  • Strategy: Analyze → Plan → Execute
  • Expertise gives you: The ability to find the right answer faster
  • What fails: Winging it. Random exploration without structure.
Complex

Unknowable in advance

  • Nature: Cause and effect only visible in retrospect
  • Examples: A market, a culture shift, a new technology’s effects
  • Strategy: Probe → Sense → RespondThe correct strategy for complex domains in Snowden’s Cynefin framework. Try something small, observe what happens, adapt. Contrast with the complicated-domain strategy of Analyze → Plan → Execute.
  • Expertise gives you: Better probes and better interpretation — but not certainty
  • What fails: Best practices. Instruction manuals. Stable rules.

In complex domains, the relationship only becomes visible after the fact, and the system shifts as you interact with it. The correct strategy for complex domains is probe, sense, respond. But the complex part — where AI actually helps your work, where it fails, where it flatters you into thinking it helped when it didn’t — that can only be discovered through direct, iterative contact with the tool. Through play.

What Play Is Here

Play, in this context, is not recreation and not games. It is a specific cognitive mode with properties that make it suited to complex domains. Four concepts define what we mean.

Experimentation without full consequences

There is a space between full reality and pure fantasy — a zone where you can test things and getting it wrong doesn’t cost much, but the results are real enough to learn from. Winnicott — Playing and Reality (1971)Winnicott called this the “transitional space” — the zone between full reality and pure fantasy where experimentation happens without full consequences. A child playing “house” is not confused about whether they’re actually a parent. They’re testing roles, behaviors, and rules where failure is information, not disaster. Applied to AI: you try a prompt, evaluate the output, adjust — in a space where a bad result teaches you about the frontier rather than costing you a deadline.. This is precisely what productive AI engagement looks like: you try a prompt, evaluate the output, adjust your approach — in a space where a bad result teaches you something about the frontier rather than costing you a deadline.

The maker-material dialogue

Skilled work develops through a dialogue between maker and material. The woodworker cuts, the wood responds — splits, resists, bends unexpectedly — the woodworker adjusts. Sennett — The Craftsman (2008)Traces this dynamic across centuries of craft practice. Skill doesn’t come from reading about wood. It comes from working with it and attending to what happens. The craftsman is not a passive recipient of the material’s properties. They actively explore, test, and shape. AI engagement follows the same structure: you prompt, the tool responds (sometimes well, sometimes badly, sometimes unexpectedly), you evaluate and adjust. AI engagement follows the same structure: you prompt, the tool responds (sometimes well, sometimes badly, sometimes in ways you didn’t expect), you evaluate and adjust. Each cycle teaches you something about the material that no manual could convey.

Agency through mastery experiences

People develop agency — the belief that their actions can produce desired outcomes — through four sources, and they are not equally powerful.

Play Solves All Three Problems

This is not a disconnected fourth section. Play is the operational response to everything in Parts 1 through 3.

Part 1 — The Enchantment
The talking library triggers mind-attribution, producing overtrust
Play forces encounters with failure. You see the tool get things wrong, produce nonsense, miss the point. Each failure is a calibration event that erodes the enchantment experientially — not because someone told you the tool has limits, but because you watched it fail.
Mechanism: calibration through failure — expectation-reality discrepancy, processed through iterative testing
Part 2 — The Invisible Frontier
AI multiplies and divides along jagged, unpredictable lines that theory can’t map
Play is frontier mapping. Each probe tests a boundary. Over repeated cycles, you build a personal map of where AI helps and where it hurts for your specific work. No course can give you this map because the frontier is domain-specific, task-specific, and model-version-specific.
Mechanism: probe-sense-respond — the only strategy that works in complex domains
Part 3 — Agency Abdication
Treating AI as if it has its own judgment, failing to structure your own
Each play cycle is a mastery experience — you set the intention, you evaluate the result, you decide what to do next. Agency develops through this repeated exercise, not through being told to “stay in the loop.”
Mechanism: mastery experiences — agency builds through successful cycles of intention-action-evaluation

No other mode does all three simultaneously. Instruction can explain the enchantment but cannot break it experientially. Best practices can describe the frontier but cannot map it for your specific work. Rules can mandate human oversight but cannot build the judgment that makes oversight meaningful.
