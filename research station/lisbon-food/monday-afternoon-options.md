# Monday Afternoon Options (before 19:00)

*Landing Monday afternoon, April 13. These are the before-dinner options.*

---

## The reframe: "lanche" is a real Portuguese meal

The 16:00–19:00 window isn't dead time in Lisbon. It's **lanche** — the afternoon snack hour. Locals sit down at a *pastelaria* with coffee and a pastry. So this window is actually a very Portuguese way to eat. You just eat differently than at dinner.

---

## Option 1 — Mercado de Campo de Ourique (the local food hall)

**The recommendation.** A neighborhood market since 1934 with a food hall added in 2013. Open **Monday 10:00–23:00**. This is Lisbon's locals' answer to Time Out Market — fewer tourists, traditional stalls mixed with a few modern names.

What's inside: a stall from **Marlene Vieira** (top Portuguese chef), **Marisqueira Azul** (seafood), a **Manteigaria** counter (same pastéis de nata as Chiado), and a *pica pau* stall (beef strips fried with pickles and spices — a classic Portuguese tapa). Wander, pick a few plates, sit at shared tables. ~€25 total for two people.

**Why this works for Monday:** Campo de Ourique is the same neighborhood as Casa dos Passarinhos (the Monday dinner pick, opens at 19:00). So the natural loop is: graze at the mercado in the late afternoon → walk to dinner when it opens. One neighborhood, no rushing.

**Getting there:** Take the famous **Tram 28** to its Campo de Ourique terminus. The tram ride is its own Lisbon experience.

Address: Rua Coelho da Rocha 104, Campo de Ourique.

---

## Option 2 — Historic pastelarias (iconic, not TikTok)

Three genuinely old, protected-heritage pastelarias where actual Lisboetas go for *lanche*:

**Confeitaria Nacional** (founded 1829) — one of the oldest pastry shops in Europe. Was literally the royal bakery. On Praça da Figueira, right next to Casa das Bifanas. Interior is ornate; locals come for lunch and afternoon snacks. Order: bolo de arroz (rice-flour muffin, everyday classic), palmier recheado (puff-pastry hearts with egg cream), bica (Lisbon espresso).

**Pastelaria Benard** (founded 1868) — on Rua Garrett in Chiado. Original aristocratic tea house, supplier to Portuguese and Italian royal houses. Next door to Manteigaria Chiado. The sit-down version of the same street.

**Pastelaria Versailles** (founded 1922) — grand Art Nouveau interior, former gathering place for Lisbon's intellectual elite. Saldanha/Avenida da República area, off the main tourist tracks. Protected by Lisbon's "Lojas com História" (Shops with History) program.

---

## Option 3 — Baixa/Chiado mini-crawl (time-sensitive)

The one item that's actively at risk because of the clock: **A Nova Pombalina** (Rua do Comércio 2) closes at **19:30**. This is the roast suckling pig sandwich (*sandes de leitão*) — cash only, 60 seconds to your hand, iconic. If you want leitão this trip, Monday afternoon is the window.

Pair with: Confeitaria Nacional (3 min walk) + Ginjinha Espinheira (5 min walk, sour cherry liqueur standing shot, since 1840, order "com elas"). A tight three-stop Baixa circuit.

---

## Option 4 — Mercado de Arroios (deep-local wildcard)

75-year-old neighborhood market in Anjos district, near Mouraria. Very local, almost no tourists. Has a hydroponic greenhouse on the terrace. Honest caveat: traditional mercados operate mornings-to-early-afternoons, so late afternoon it may not be at full tilt. Fewer food stalls, more produce/fish/butcher. Riskier choice — Campo de Ourique is the safer food-hall bet.

---

## The recommendation

**Tram 28 → Mercado de Campo de Ourique (graze 16:30–18:30) → Casa dos Passarinhos for dinner at 19:00.** Everything stays in one neighborhood, it's not touristy, the tram ride is its own experience, and you get both a food-hall grazing session AND a proper sit-down tasca dinner without rushing.

The alternative: **Baixa/Chiado crawl → Nova Pombalina for leitão before 19:30 → dinner elsewhere.** More variety, hits the leitão sandwich, but more walking and neighborhood-hopping on a jet-lag afternoon.
