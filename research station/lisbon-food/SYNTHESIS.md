## Executive Summary (v2 — FINAL, after Round 2)

**The shape of it:** Lisbon in April is perfect for bifanas, pastéis de nata, and tascas. Sardines are out (wrong season, wait until August). Snails are just beginning — watch for "Há caracóis!" signs but don't chase them. The real move is: hit **Mouraria** and **Campo de Ourique** for tascas, do a **bifana + ginjinha + pastel crawl** in Baixa/Chiado, and know that **Monday closes a lot of the best places** — plan around it.

**⚠️ MONDAY WARNING (the research's most important finding):**
Three of the most-recommended spots are **CLOSED ON MONDAY**: Cervejaria Ramiro, O Velho Eurico, Zé dos Cornos. If Monday is a core eating day, you need to work around this.

**The anchors:**

1. **Zé da Mouraria** (Mouraria) — the taxi drivers' tasca. Cash only, lunch only (12-4pm, Mon-Sat, **OPEN MONDAY**), no reservations. Get there by 12:00-12:15 or wait an hour. Order: bacalhau à minhota. This is the purest "non-flashy but famous for one thing" pick in the city, endorsed by Lisbon's top chefs. Rua João do Outeiro 24.

2. **Imperial de Campo de Ourique** aka **Tasca do João** — family-run since 1985, "Comércio com História" designation. Chanfana (goat stew) is the signature; menu rotates by day. **OPEN MONDAY** (Mon-Sat 10am-5pm, closed Sundays). Rua Correia Teles 67.

3. **Casa dos Passarinhos** (Campo de Ourique, since 1923) — choco frito (fried cuttlefish) "as good as Setúbal," naco na pedra (steak on stone). **OPEN MONDAY** lunch AND dinner. No reservations. Rua Silva Carvalho 195.

4. **Marisqueira do Lis** — the actual Ramiro Monday-alternative. 250m from Ramiro, open since 1973, **OPEN MONDAY** (closed Tuesdays — the inverse of Ramiro). Gambas ao alho, percebes. Av. Almirante Reis 27B.

5. **The bifana / leitão / pastel / ginjinha walking crawl in Baixa-Chiado** (all OPEN MONDAY):
   - **Casa das Bifanas** (Praça da Figueira 7A) — 6:30am–midnight. Generations of Lisboetas.
   - **A Nova Pombalina** (Rua do Comércio 2) — 7:30am-7:30pm Mon-Sat. THE leitão sandwich spot, 60 seconds to your hand, cash only.
   - **Manteigaria Chiado** (Rua do Loreto 2) — 8am-midnight. The pastel-de-nata answer that isn't Belém.
   - **Ginjinha Espinheira** (Largo São Domingos 8) — standing-only sour-cherry shot, 1840, "com elas" (with a cherry).
   - **A Tendinha do Rossio** — pastéis de bacalhau, crispy, old-school.

**Secret-weapon local picks** (Portuguese-press recommendations, not on English travel blogs):
- **Tasca do Miguel** (Areeiro, Rua Egas Moniz 45A) — won the national "Tascando" best-tasca vote. 30 years. 150 meals/day to neighborhood residents. Call 218404657 for hours (not verified for Monday).
- **O Trigueirinho** (Mouraria back alleys) — hidden family tasca, basically unknown in English.
- **Stop do Bairro** (Campo de Ourique, Rua Tenente Ferreira Durão 55A) — soccer memorabilia walls, typed-daily-specials menu in Portuguese.

**For Tue–Sat (if your trip extends beyond Monday):**
- **Zé dos Cornos** (Mouraria) — charcoal pork ribs, €13 platter, wait in the street with a beer. Tue-Sat.
- **O Velho Eurico** (Mouraria) — neo-tasca, blackboard menu, polvo à lagareiro (octopus) is the standout. Reservation weeks ahead via Instagram DM; or walk-in at opening.
- **Cervejaria Ramiro** (Av. Almirante Reis 1H) — the famous seafood+beer spot. Still genuinely good food even if "touristier than it used to be." Finish with a prego for dessert.

**Hard skips:**
- Sardines (wait until August)
- Casa Portuguesa do Pastel de Bacalhau (tourist trap with premium pricing)
- Time Out Market (overpriced, crowded, everything is better at its source)
- Any restaurant with photos of food outside, laminated menus in 6 languages, or a waiter calling you in
- Anything on Rua Augusta or around Praça do Comércio
- Restaurant Sol Nascente (Alfama, Rua S. Tome) — the €9.60-for-bread scam

**Couvert rule:** Say "Não, obrigado" to bread/cheese/olives if you don't want them. Once you take a bite you pay for everything. ~€2/person is normal; >€5 = walk out.

## Findings

### Round 1 — Mission 1 (Street food) — Batch 1

#### 1. Bifana is THE Lisbon street sandwich
Quote: "The bifana is Lisbon's ultimate on-the-go street food — a sandwich made from thin pork slices marinated in garlic, white wine and paprika, then grilled and tucked into a crusty bread roll." (eatingeurope.com)
Locals add mustard or piri-piri. Confidence: HIGH (multiple sources).

#### 2. Best bifana spots (by name)
- **O Trevo** — Praça Luís Camões. Anthony Bourdain called them "hot, porky love." (devourtours.com)
- **Casa das Bifanas** — Praça da Figueira. "No-nonsense eatery where generations of Lisboetas have stopped for their daily fix. Soft, juicy, perfectly seasoned — tangy garlicky sauce." (wheretolx.com)
- **Café Beira Gare** — near Rossio station. Old-school, bifanas put it on the map.
- **As Bifanas do Afonso** — standing-room only, €2 bifana on crusty bread, locals' pick. (devourtours.com)
- **O Triângulo da Ribeira** — behind Mercado da Ribeira, Cais do Sodré, four bifana variants.
Confidence: HIGH — overlapping recommendations across 4+ sources.

#### 3. Pastel de nata — skip (or don't skip) Belém
Quote: "The best Pastel de Nata in Lisbon comes from Manteigaria... perfectly crispy and flaky pastry enveloping a lusciously sweet, creamy, and gooey custard filling." (etfoodvoyage.com)
- **Manteigaria** — Chiado flagship is the non-Belém pick. Watch them roll and cream the tarts through glass.
- **Pastelaria Aloma** — won Lisbon's annual blind competition in 2012, 2013, 2015.
- **Castro** — Rua Garrett. Alice-in-Wonderland design, quieter line.
- "Rua Garrett = the Pastel Promenade" — 4 top-tier shops within 200m. Useful if the Manteigaria line is out the door.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 4. Ginjinha — sour cherry liqueur, served at a standing counter
Quote: "A Ginjinha Espinheira is a tiny bar near Rossio Square, at Largo de São Domingos, opened in 1840, and claims to be the very first to sell the cherry liqueur commercially... always a crowd of semi-inebriated tourists and Portuguese stood around outside." (tripadvisor.com / lisbonguide.org)
- Order "com elas" (with a cherry at the bottom) for the full experience.
- Same-family-run for five generations.
- Also: **Ginjinha Sem Rival** and **Ginjinha Rubi** nearby on Rua das Portas de Santo Antão for the 1€ standing-shot circuit.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 5. Caracóis (snails) — the season just opened
Quote: "Snails are a seasonal product available from as early as April to as late as September... countless cafes and homestyle tascas put out signs reading 'Há caracóis!'" (theculturetrip.com, getlisbon.com)
- You're arriving right at the start of snail season. Look for handwritten "Há caracóis!" signs on tasca doors.
- Served with olive oil, garlic, piri-piri, lots of oregano. Suck them from the shell; beer on the side.
- **Caracóis de São Bento** — "really good reputation." Also Casa dos Caracóis, O Rei do Caracóis.
- Early in the season they may be small — quality peaks in May-August.
Confidence: HIGH for season timing and method, MEDIUM for specific April quality.

#### 6. Cervejaria Ramiro — seafood + prego-for-dessert
Quote: "Cervejaria Ramiro has been serving up the best seafood in the city since 1956... old-fashioned beer and seafood restaurant... loud and convivial, not the type of place where you're expected to gingerly shell your prawns with a knife and fork." (willflyforfood.net, 2foodtrippers.com)
- Tradition: "finish a seafood meal with a meat sandwich" — order the **prego no pão** as dessert after the seafood. Weird but signature.
- **Caveat for the devil's advocate mission**: Ramiro is world-famous and lines are brutal. Need to check if it's still "home-cooked vibe" or slipped into hype territory.
Confidence: HIGH on history, FLAG for hype check.

### Surprising / Contradictory

- Bifana spots cluster around Rossio/Figueira/Baixa — walking distance between them makes a bifana crawl possible in one afternoon.

### Round 1 — Mission 1 (Street food) — Batch 2

#### 7. Pastel de bacalhau (cod fritter) — best spots
- **A Tendinha do Rossio** — "crispy on the outside, perfectly seasoned cod inside, affordable salgados €1.50-3, traditional preparation." (cityguidelisbon.com) Tiny standing-bar near Rossio. Looks like the sweet spot: cheap, old-school, not a tourist trap.
- **O Beco** (Alfama) — "among the finest places for bacalhau, impressive pastéis de bacalhau — hearty with a rich potato base and thick batter." (eatingeurope.com)
- **As Salgadeiras** (Bairro Alto) — former neighborhood bakery, "high ceilings and a homely vibe."
- **Casa Portuguesa do Pastel de Bacalhau** (Rua Augusta): "modern twist by adding Queijo da Serra cheese... tourist-oriented spot with premium pricing." → **AVOID** if you want authenticity. Multiple sources flag it as not traditional.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 8. Grilled sardines — April is too early, basically
Quote: "Sardines are in season only from March/April to September/October, BUT... locals know the best, freshest sardines arrive in July and August." (devourtours.com, tingslisbon.com)
- **Verdict: Skip sardines this trip.** April is technically the start but they won't be at their best. Locals' saying: "wait until August."
- If you really want them: Floresta das Escadinhas (Alfama) specializes year-round.
- **Better seasonal replacement in April**: cavala (mackerel), carapau (horse mackerel), dourada (sea bream), or the start of snail season.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 9. Sandes de leitão — Lisbon's roast suckling pig sandwich
Quote: "The sandes de leitão is where tradition meets indulgence. Nothing but the bun and pieces of meat... at the most a little gravy for dipping... Leitão is roasted suckling pig basted in herbs and spices on a spit over a wood fire." (takewalks.com, tasteoflisboa.com)
- **A Nova Pombalina** (Rua do Comércio, Baixa) — famous for their leitão sandwich, visible in the window display. Classic no-frills lunch counter.
- **Afonso dos Leitões** — also top-rated for leitão.
- Leitão from Negrais (the village 30min from Lisbon) is the gold standard.
- More expensive than bifana/prego because of the labor involved; it's a treat not a daily sandwich.
Confidence: HIGH.

### Mission 1 — Closing notes
- Street food stack for Lisbon in April: **bifana + pastel de nata + ginjinha + pastel de bacalhau** is the reliable core.
- **Opportunistic**: snails IF you see "há caracóis!" signs (possible but early), leitão sandwich as a splurge.
- **Skip**: sardines (wrong season), Casa Portuguesa do Pastel de Bacalhau (tourist trap).
- **Walk circuit**: Rossio / Praça da Figueira / Rua Garrett / Largo de São Domingos are all within 10 minutes of each other → possible to hit 4-5 classic spots in one afternoon.

### Round 1 — Mission 2 (Tascas & hole-in-the-wall classics) — Batch 1

#### 10. Zé da Mouraria — the taxi drivers' tasca
Quote: "A favourite amongst Lisbon's taxi drivers... the codfish is a must here... bacalhau à minhota (roasted cod with chickpeas and mashed potatoes)... portions intended for two people easily serving three to four." (afar.com, tripadvisor.com)

- **Cash only**, no cards.
- **No reservations** — by 12:45 there was "a waiting time of at least an hour" so arrive by 12:00 or 12:15.
- **Dish to order**: Bacalhau à Minhota. Portions are huge — share.
- Mouraria neighborhood (birthplace of fado).
- **This is the clearest "non-flashy but famous for one thing" hit.** Multiple top chefs call it the best tasca in the city.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 11. O Velho Eurico — the neo-tasca with a month-long waitlist
Quote: "A neo-tasca revamp by chef Zé Paulo Rocha, one of the toughest Lisbon reservations to score... seasonal blackboard menu features classic dishes like bacalhau à brás and arroz de pato plus regional dishes like migas d'alheira." (2foodtrippers.com, theinfatuation.com)
- Mouraria, on the way up to Castelo São Jorge.
- **Reservation needed weeks ahead** via their Instagram DMs. Without one: 1–2 hour wait.
- **Dish to order**: polvo à lagareiro (octopus) is the standout; also bacalhau à brás, arroz de pato, "squirty fries."
- ~€25/person.
- **Flag**: this is "neo-tasca" not original-tasca. Vibe is still casual but the cooking is chef-driven. Worth knowing before you go — it's not a cozinha familiar grandma spot.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 12. Zé dos Cornos — the grilled-meat line-out-the-door
Quote: "There's always a line at Zé dos Cornos but they'll happily serve you beers while you wait on the street. Share a rack straight off the charcoal grill for around €13." (oladaniela.com)
- **Mouraria** (a theme is emerging — Mouraria is the tasca neighborhood).
- **Dish to order**: the charcoal-grilled pork ribs, with tomato salad and bean rice.
- Family-run, third generation (José Ferreira's son).
- €13 a platter, shareable.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 13. More tasca picks with specialties

- **O Tasco do Vigário** (Alfama, east end on a historic street) — genuine hole-in-the-wall, relaxed.
- **O Triângulo da Ribeira** (Bairro Alto) — "40 years of thick-cut perfectly cooked pork," local lunch favorite.
- **Tasca Baldracca** (Mouraria) — caldeirada de peixe (fish stew).
- Shrimp soup tasca mentioned without name — flag to resolve in Round 2.
Confidence: MEDIUM on the last one, HIGH on the named ones.

### How to spot an authentic tasca — rules of thumb
- Paper-menu-only, Portuguese only, scrawled daily = good sign.
- Old neighborhoods, away from tourist streets.
- Big portions, €10-15 for a full plate.
- House specialty + rotating daily specials that may repeat weekly.

### Surprising
- **Mouraria is the tasca capital**, not Alfama. Three of the best spots (Zé da Mouraria, O Velho Eurico, Zé dos Cornos) are all in Mouraria. This is a useful concentration — you could eat breakfast, lunch, and dinner in a 10-min walk.
- Zé da Mouraria is cash-only + lunch-only + no reservations. Planning matters.
- O Velho Eurico needs reservations weeks ahead — probably too late for Monday. Plan B: show up at opening or go during off-peak lunch hours.

### Round 1 — Mission 3 (Devil's Advocate) — Batch 1

#### 18. Hard skip: restaurants on Rua Augusta and Praça do Comércio
Quote: "Restaurants around Comercio Square are the definition of tourist traps with mediocre food at premium prices, served with a side of pushy waiters and laminated menus in six languages." (explorespy.com, atlaslisboa.com)
- **Specific red flags** to scan for: photos of dishes displayed outside, staff inviting you in from the street, identical menus next door, menus in 6 languages.
- **Rule**: if a host is outside calling you in, walk away.
- **Streets to avoid for food**: Rua Augusta, Rua dos Correeiros, Praça do Comércio perimeter, and the tourist half of Rua das Portas de Santo Antão (the "restaurant row" with the waiters grabbing people).
Confidence: HIGH — consistent across 5+ sources.

#### 19. Couvert trap — know the rule
Quote: "When you enter a restaurant, you are immediately given bread, olives, cheese and butter... you pay extra for them... ~€2/person is normal, <€5 at better places." (myportugalholiday.com, weheartlisbon.com)
- **How to refuse**: say "Não, obrigado" and push them back. They'll be removed.
- **Once you take a single bite, you're paying for everything.**
- **Specific scam**: Restaurant Sol Nascente on Rua S. Tome in Alfama charged €9.60 for bread — avoid. Any couvert >€5/person at a casual place = walking out is justified.
- Couvert itself is NOT a scam — it's a Portuguese custom. The scam is overpriced couvert at tourist spots.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 20. Time Out Market — the nuanced take
Quote: "NOT a tourist trap but definitely more popular with people visiting Lisbon than locals... Quality-wise falls a mile below the rest of Lisbon and is 3 times the price... monotone food stalls, bench tables, crowds with trays looking for places to sit." (rustyfood.com, 2foodtrippers.com)
- **Verdict for this trip**: SKIP — you've explicitly said you don't want Time Out. The research confirms your instinct: it's not bad, but everything served there is better and cheaper at its source.
- Exception: Manteigaria has a counter at Time Out — same tarts, shorter line than Chiado.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 21. Pastéis de Belém vs. Manteigaria — the long-lines question
Quote: "Despite the long lines... service moves surprisingly fast... don't be daunted by the queues; head straight INSIDE where there's plenty of seating for dine-in service." (everysteph.com, aportugueseaffair.com)
- **The takeaway line is misleading** — there's a separate entrance for sit-down service that most tourists miss. Queue inside = wait 5 min vs 45 min.
- **Is Belém worth it?** Only if you're already in Belém seeing the Monastery and Tower. The pastry itself is arguably tied with Manteigaria. Many locals prefer Manteigaria.
- **The honest comparison**: Manteigaria = slightly crispier, slightly less sweet, no wait. Belém = secret-recipe (the only place using the 1837 original) but the pilgrimage factor is the real reason to go.
Confidence: HIGH.

#### 22. Fado "tourist" restaurants in Alfama — don't eat at them
- Rule of thumb: any restaurant in Alfama advertising "Fado show + dinner" with a set menu is primarily in the fado-show business. Food is afterthought. Go for the fado, not the food — or better: see fado at a dedicated venue and eat separately.
- Quote: "Unfortunately including Alfama and Graça [are tourist-heavy]... the restaurants on the top tourist streets are tourist traps 99% of the time." (wheretolx.com)
- **Better option** flagged in Mission 2: Santa Rita (Alfama) — tucked away, not on a tourist drag.

### Mission 3 — Closing notes
- **Core rules to apply on Monday**:
  1. No photos of food outside.
  2. No host calling you in from the street.
  3. Menu in Portuguese first (and ideally only).
  4. Prato do dia written on paper or chalkboard, not laminated.
  5. If it's on Rua Augusta, walk away.
  6. Couvert: refuse if you don't want it; check the price if you accept.
- **Spots to avoid by name**: Restaurant Sol Nascente (Alfama), anything around Comércio Square, Casa Portuguesa do Pastel de Bacalhau (Rua Augusta).
- **Ramiro is NOT blacklisted** — it's still good. Just understand you're eating at a now-famous spot with a queue.
